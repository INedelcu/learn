Next-event estimation & light/BSDF MIS

24_nee_mis.cpp — to light a point you can sample the BSDF and hope to hit a light, or sample a point on the light directly. Each is catastrophic in the scene that suits the other; MIS combines them and is robust to both.

Direct lighting is an integral of incoming light times the BRDF. Sampling the BSDF (example 19) and following the ray works for big soft lights but almost never hits a small bright one — mostly zeros, rare spikes. Next-event estimation fixes that by sampling a point on the light and connecting to it — but it fails the opposite way, spraying samples across a large light reflected in a near-mirror. Neither wins everywhere, which is precisely what MIS (example 09) was built for. This is Veach's canonical result, applied to the two strategies a real renderer combines.

One-sentence summary. The direct-lighting integral can be sampled two ways — over the light's solid angle (next-event estimation, pdf $r^2/(A\cos\theta_\ell)$ from example 23) or by BSDF/VNDF sampling (pdf $G_1 D/4(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)$ from example 19) — and which one wins flips with the scene; combining them per-sample with the balance heuristic $w_i=p_i/\sum_j p_j$ keeps the estimator unbiased and within a small factor of whichever strategy is best, never the disastrous one.

1. One integral, two ways to sample it

The light reaching a shading point from an area light is an integral over the light's surface — the BRDF, the light's emission, and the two cosines and inverse-square that make up the geometry term:

$$L=\int_{\text{light}} f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,L_e\,\cos\theta_i\,\frac{\cos\theta_\ell}{r^2}\,dA.$$

There are two natural importance strategies, and they sample the same integral in different measures:

  • Light sampling (NEE): pick a point on the light (uniform on its area, $p_A=1/A$), and convert to a solid-angle pdf with the example-23 factor $p_\ell(\omega_i)=r^2/(A\cos\theta_\ell)$. Every sample lands on the light, so it is never wasted on darkness — ideal for small lights.
  • BSDF sampling: draw a direction $\omega_i$ from the GGX VNDF (example 19), pdf $p_b(\omega_i)=G_1(\omega_o)D(h)/\bigl(4(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)\bigr)$, and check whether it happens to hit the light. Every sample follows the lobe, so it is ideal when the lobe is sharp and the light is large.

Because both pdfs are expressed in the same solid-angle measure, they can be compared and combined — the prerequisite for MIS.

3. MIS combines them — and is robust to both

MIS (example 09) takes one sample from each strategy and weights them so neither spike survives. With the light's pdf $p_\ell$ and the BSDF's pdf $p_b$ both in solid-angle measure, the balance heuristic weight for the light sample is $w_\ell=p_\ell/(p_\ell+p_b)$ and for the BSDF sample $w_b=p_b/(p_b+p_\ell)$, each pdf evaluated at its own sample — exactly the example-09 construction, unbiased for the same reason proved there. Read the variance column and the whole point appears: the best single strategy flips — BSDF in the first scene (light sampling is $201\times$ worse), light in the second (BSDF is $26{,}967\times$ worse) — and you don't know in advance which a given pixel needs. MIS is within $3.5\times$ of the best in scene 1 and essentially equal to it in scene 2, never the catastrophic one. The power heuristic ($w_i\propto p_i^2$) pushes the losing technique's weight down harder than the balance heuristic, trimming MIS's small overhead over the best.

4. In the renderer

This is exactly how production direct lighting works, and the natural next step for the PathTracingDemo renderer. Its single-scattering estimators already sample a light directly (the NEE half) and sample the BRDF lobe (the continuation half); MIS-weighting those two with the balance heuristic — light pdf vs BSDF pdf, both in solid angle — is the upgrade that makes glossy surfaces under area lights converge. It needs the light's solid-angle pdf (example 23), the VNDF pdf (example 19), and the weighting rule (example 09): every piece is already in the series.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 24_nee_mis.cpp.

One light sample and one BSDF sample per iteration; the balance heuristic weights each by its own pdf over the sum of both pdfs at that direction.

// LIGHT sample: uniform point on the disk -> solid-angle pdf r^2/(A cos) (ex. 23)
Vec3 P = sampleDiskUniform(L);  Vec3 wiL = normalize(P);
double pL = (rL*rL*rL) / (PI * L.R*L.R * L.H);          // r^2 / (A cos_light)

// BSDF sample: VNDF direction -> pdf G1 D / (4 n·wo) (ex. 19); may miss the light
Vec3 wiB = GgxReflect(wo, SampleGgxVNDF(wo, a, u1, u2));
double pB = BsdfPdf(wo, wiB, a);

// balance heuristic: weight each sample by its own pdf / (sum of both pdfs)
double pB_atL = BsdfPdf(wo, wiL, a);                    // the OTHER pdf at this dir
double pL_atB = LightPdfSA(rB, L);
double wL = pL / (pL + pB_atL);
double wB = pB / (pB + pL_atB);
est = wL * Contribution(wo,wiL) / pL  +  wB * Contribution(wo,wiB) / pB;
// power heuristic: replace p by p^2 in the weights -> suppresses the loser harder
Takeaway. Direct lighting is one integral with two good importance strategies — sample the light (NEE), or sample the BSDF — and which is better flips with roughness and light size, unpredictably across an image. MIS takes one of each and weights them by $w_i=p_i/\sum_j p_j$ (balance) or $p_i^2/\sum_j p_j^2$ (power), staying within a small factor of the best strategy and never falling into the catastrophic one. It is example 09's heuristic applied to the light and BSDF pdfs of examples 23 and 19 — the backbone of production direct lighting.
