Control variates: subtract a known integral

27_control_variates.cpp — importance sampling reshapes where you sample; control variates leave the sampling alone and instead subtract a cheap function whose integral you already know, cancelling the part of the integrand it can predict.

Importance sampling (example 06) was one big variance-reduction lever. Control variates are the other, and they are orthogonal to it: keep sampling however you like, but find a cheap "control" function $g$ whose integral $G$ you know in closed form and which is correlated with the integrand $f$. Subtracting $g-G$ (which averages to zero, so the estimate is unchanged) cancels exactly the fluctuation of $f$ that $g$ predicts — and the better $g$ tracks $f$, the more variance disappears.

One-sentence summary. To estimate $I=\int f$, pick a control $g$ with known integral $G=\int g$ and form $\hat I=\overline{f(X)-c\,(g(X)-G)}$; since $\mathbb E[g-G]=0$ it is unbiased for any $c$, and the optimal $c^\star=\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)/\operatorname{Var}(g)$ shrinks the variance by the factor $1/(1-\rho^2)$ where $\rho=\operatorname{corr}(f,g)$ — here $g(x)=1+x$ tracks $e^x$ with $\rho=0.992$, cutting variance $61\times$.

1. Subtract something you can integrate exactly

Suppose you want $I=\int f$ and you happen to know the exact integral $G=\int g$ of some other function. Because $\mathbb E[g(X)-G]=0$ when $X$ is uniform, you can subtract any multiple of it without changing the answer:

$$\hat I=\frac1N\sum_i\Big(f(X_i)-c\,\big(g(X_i)-G\big)\Big),\qquad \mathbb E[\hat I]=\mathbb E[f]-c\,\underbrace{(\mathbb E[g]-G)}_{=\,0}=I.$$

Unbiased for every constant $c$. The point is what it does to the spread: $g-G$ is a known-mean stand-in for $f$'s wiggle, and subtracting it removes whatever part of that wiggle $g$ shares with $f$. If $g$ closely tracks $f$, the leftover $f-c(g-G)$ is nearly flat — and a nearly-flat integrand has nearly-zero variance. Here $f(x)=e^x$ on $[0,1]$ and the control is the line $g(x)=1+x$ (integral $G=1.5$); slide $c$ and watch the residual flatten:

2. The optimal amount, and the $1/(1-\rho^2)$ collapse

Which $c$ is best? The per-sample variance is a quadratic in $c$:

$$\operatorname{Var}\!\big[f-c(g-G)\big]=\operatorname{Var}(f)-2c\,\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)+c^2\operatorname{Var}(g).$$

Minimize over $c$ — set the derivative $-2\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)+2c\operatorname{Var}(g)$ to zero:

$$\boxed{\,c^\star=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)}{\operatorname{Var}(g)}\,}$$

Substituting $c^\star$ back collapses the quadratic to its minimum, and writing $\rho=\operatorname{corr}(f,g)=\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)/\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(f)\operatorname{Var}(g)}$ it becomes

$$\operatorname{Var}\!\big[f-c^\star(g-G)\big]=\operatorname{Var}(f)-\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)^2}{\operatorname{Var}(g)}=\operatorname{Var}(f)\,(1-\rho^2).$$

So the variance drops by the factor $1/(1-\rho^2)$ — the entire payoff rides on the correlation. A control with $\rho=0.9$ cuts variance $5.3\times$; $\rho=0.99$ cuts it $50\times$; $\rho=0.999$, $500\times$. The parabola makes the optimum visible:

The pilot finds $\rho=0.99182$, predicting $1/(1-\rho^2)=61.4\times$ — and the measured reduction is $61.4\times$, dead on. Both estimators agree on $e-1=1.71828$ (unbiased); the control variate simply removed almost all of $e^x$'s spread, because the line $1+x$ already explains $99.2\%$ of it. The remaining $0.8\%$ — the curvature the line can't follow — is all that's left to integrate.

4. In the renderer

Control variates are the principle behind "subtract an analytic approximation, integrate only the residual." When part of the light transport has a closed form — a constant or low-order-spherical-harmonic ambient term, an analytic area-light approximation, the previous frame's denoised estimate — a renderer can compute that part exactly and Monte-Carlo only the difference, which carries far less variance. It composes with importance sampling: one reshapes the sampling density, the other subtracts a known baseline, and they multiply their gains. The catch is finding a control that is both cheap and well-correlated — when $\rho$ is small the factor $1/(1-\rho^2)\approx1$ and there is nothing to gain.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 27_control_variates.cpp.

Estimate $c^\star$ from a pilot, then subtract $c^\star(g-G)$ from each sample.

// pilot: accumulate Var(g) and Cov(f,g) (Welford), then c* = Cov / Var(g)
double cStar = cfg / cgg;                         // Cov(f,g) / Var(g)

// the two estimators, same draws:
double plain = f(x);                              // c = 0
double ctrl  = f(x) - cStar * (g(x) - G);         // subtract the known-mean control
// E[ctrl] = E[f] - c*(E[g]-G) = I, since E[g] = G  ->  unbiased for any c.
// Var[ctrl] = Var(f) * (1 - rho^2), minimized at c = c*.
Takeaway. If you know the exact integral $G$ of a function $g$ correlated with your integrand $f$, subtract $c(g-G)$: it changes nothing in expectation (mean-zero) but cancels the shared fluctuation. The best amount is $c^\star=\operatorname{Cov}(f,g)/\operatorname{Var}(g)$, and it cuts variance by $1/(1-\rho^2)$ — $61\times$ here, where the line $1+x$ explains $99\%$ of $e^x$. It is a variance-reduction lever orthogonal to importance sampling, and the basis of "integrate only the residual" in renderers.
