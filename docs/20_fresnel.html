Fresnel reflectance: exact equations vs Schlick

20_fresnel.cpp — every surface turns mirror-bright at grazing angles. That ramp is the Fresnel term that multiplies essentially every BRDF; the renderer evaluates it with one cheap power of Schlick's approximation.

Look at the rim of any sphere, or a road under a low sun: the surface reflects far more light where you see it edge-on than head-on. That angular ramp is Fresnel reflectance $F(\theta)$ — the fraction of light reflected rather than transmitted, as a function of the angle to the surface normal. It is a multiplier on the specular term of every microfacet BRDF, evaluated on every shading sample. The exact equations come from electromagnetics; Schlick's approximation replaces them with a single $(1-\cos\theta)^5$. This page derives where both come from, and measures exactly how close Schlick gets.

One-sentence summary. Fresnel ramps reflectance from $F_0$ at normal incidence up to $1$ at grazing; the exact value averages two polarizations of the electromagnetic boundary conditions (a function of the refractive indices), while Schlick approximates the whole curve as $F(\theta)=F_0+(1-F_0)(1-\cos\theta)^5$ — exact at both endpoints by construction, within a few percent in between, needing only the single number $F_0$ (from the IOR for dielectrics, from the base color for metals).

1. The grazing-angle ramp

At normal incidence ($\theta=0$, looking straight down at the surface) only a little light reflects — for glass, about $4\%$. As the view tilts toward grazing ($\theta\to90°$) the reflectance climbs, reaching $100\%$ exactly at the horizon for every material. That is why still water is nearly transparent overhead but a perfect mirror in the distance. The curve below is $F(\theta)$; pick a material and compare the exact reflectance against Schlick's one-line fit.

2. The exact equations, and where $F_0$ comes from

Light is an electromagnetic wave, and the boundary conditions at a flat interface split it into two polarizations — parallel and perpendicular to the plane of incidence — that reflect by different amounts. For a dielectric (glass, water) with indices $n_i$ (incident side) and $n_t$ (transmitted side), with $\cos\theta_t$ from Snell's law, the reflectances are

$$r_\parallel=\frac{n_t\cos\theta_i-n_i\cos\theta_t}{n_t\cos\theta_i+n_i\cos\theta_t},\qquad r_\perp=\frac{n_i\cos\theta_i-n_t\cos\theta_t}{n_i\cos\theta_i+n_t\cos\theta_t},\qquad F=\tfrac12\big(r_\parallel^2+r_\perp^2\big),$$

the $\tfrac12$ averaging the two polarizations for unpolarized light. A conductor (metal) uses the same construction but with a complex index $n+i\,k$; the absorption coefficient $k$ is what makes metals both highly reflective and tinted.

Where $F_0$ comes from. Schlick needs only the reflectance at normal incidence. Set $\theta_i=0$, so $\cos\theta_i=\cos\theta_t=1$, and the dielectric formulas collapse: $r_\parallel=\dfrac{n_t-n_i}{n_t+n_i}$ and $r_\perp=-\dfrac{n_t-n_i}{n_t+n_i}$ — equal in magnitude — so both squares are the same and

$$F_0=\left(\frac{n_t-n_i}{n_t+n_i}\right)^2\ \text{(dielectric)},\qquad F_0=\frac{(n-1)^2+k^2}{(n+1)^2+k^2}\ \text{(conductor)} .$$

Glass into air ($n_i=1,\,n_t=1.5$) gives $F_0=(0.5/2.5)^2=0.04$ — the famous "4% spec" of dielectrics. The metal here ($n=0.2,\,k=3$) gives $F_0=(0.8^2+9)/(1.2^2+9)=9.64/10.44=0.923$: most light reflects even head-on, which is what "looks metallic" means.

3. Schlick's approximation

Snell's law, two polarizations, a square root, a complex index — too much for a value computed millions of times per frame. Schlick (1994) replaces all of it with one interpolation between $F_0$ and $1$, steered by a fifth power of $(1-\cos\theta)$:

$$\boxed{\,F(\theta)=F_0+(1-F_0)\,(1-\cos\theta)^5\,}$$

The only material input is $F_0$. And it is exact at both endpoints by construction: at normal incidence $\cos\theta=1$, so $(1-\cos\theta)^5=0$ and $F=F_0$; at grazing $\cos\theta=0$, so $(1-\cos\theta)^5=1$ and $F=F_0+(1-F_0)=1$. The fifth power is chosen so the ramp in between hugs the true curve — the rest of this page checks how well.

Read off the two stories. Glass: error is essentially zero up to $45°$, peaks at $0.036$ in the steep grazing ramp near $85°$, then closes again to $0$ at the horizon — a worst case of under four reflectance points, invisible in practice. Metal: the error peaks earlier (at $75°$, $0.015$) because the exact conductor curve dips below $F_0$ before it rises — reflectance falls from $0.923$ at $0°$ to $0.918$ at $60°$ — and Schlick, which only ever increases, cannot reproduce that dip. That structural mismatch, not the size of $F_0$, is why conductors are Schlick's looser case.

5. See it: the Fresnel rim

On a sphere viewed head-on, the angle to the normal grows from $0°$ at the center to $90°$ at the silhouette — so a sphere shaded purely by $F$ is the Fresnel curve wrapped onto a ball. The center sits at $F_0$ (near-black for glass), and the edge flares to $1$: the bright rim light you see on glass and ceramics. 6. In the renderer

The PathTracingDemo renderer uses Schlick everywhere it needs reflectance: Utils.hlsl carries the Schlick helpers for the opaque and transparent (refraction) cases, and the material setup in PathTracingStandard.shader feeds them an $F_0$ built from the IOR and _SpecularColor for dielectrics, and from the base color for metals — exactly the two $F_0$ formulas of §2. That single $F_0$ is then all Schlick needs to reproduce the whole angular ramp.

This $F$ is the same factor that rode along, unevaluated, in the GGX BRDF of example 19: there we set $F=1$ to isolate the geometry, and the VNDF weight came out $F\,G_2/G_1$. Putting the real Schlick $F$ back into that weight is how the renderer tints and angle-modulates every glossy reflection.

7. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 20_fresnel.cpp.

Schlick is one line; the only input is $F_0$, computed once per material from the indices. The exact dielectric (shown for reference) is the polarization average.

// Schlick: one pow() in place of the electromagnetics.
double Schlick(double cosi, double F0) {
    double m = 1.0 - cosi;
    return F0 + (1.0 - F0) * (m*m) * (m*m) * m;        // F0 + (1-F0)(1-cos)^5
}

// F0 at normal incidence — the only number Schlick needs:
double F0_dielectric = pow((nt - ni) / (nt + ni), 2.0);              // glass 1.5 -> 0.04
double F0_conductor  = ((n-1)*(n-1) + k*k) / ((n+1)*(n+1) + k*k);    // from complex index n+ik

// exact unpolarized dielectric, for the comparison:
double sint = ni/nt * sqrt(fmax(0.0, 1.0 - cosi*cosi));   // Snell
if (sint >= 1.0) return 1.0;                              // total internal reflection
double cost = sqrt(fmax(0.0, 1.0 - sint*sint));
double rpar = (nt*cosi - ni*cost) / (nt*cosi + ni*cost);
double rper = (ni*cosi - nt*cost) / (ni*cosi + nt*cost);
return 0.5 * (rpar*rpar + rper*rper);                     // average the two polarizations
Takeaway. Fresnel is the angular ramp that brightens every surface from $F_0$ at normal incidence to $1$ at grazing. The exact value averages two polarizations of the electromagnetic boundary conditions; Schlick replaces that with $F_0+(1-F_0)(1-\cos\theta)^5$ — exact at both endpoints, within $0.036$ for glass and $0.015$ for the metal in between, for the price of one power. It is the reflectance multiplier the renderer applies on top of every specular sample, with $F_0$ read from the IOR or the base color.
