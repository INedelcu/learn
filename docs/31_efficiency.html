Efficiency: variance × time, not variance alone

31_efficiency.cpp — every earlier example scored techniques by variance. But a method that halves the variance is worthless if it runs ten times slower. The honest figure of merit is efficiency: 1 / (variance × time).

This series has compared techniques by variance throughout. That is only half the ledger. A sampler that cuts variance in half but costs ten times as much per sample is a bad trade — in the same wall-clock time the simple method draws ten times as many samples and ends up ahead. The fair comparison fixes the time budget, and the figure of merit that captures it is efficiency (Veach): one over variance times cost. A clever estimator that is too slow loses to brute force.

One-sentence summary. For a fixed time budget $T$ you can afford $n=T/t$ samples (cost $t$ each), so the estimator's error variance is $V/n=V\,t/T$ — meaning the quantity to minimize is the product $V\!\cdot\!t$, and the figure of merit is efficiency $\varepsilon=1/(V\,t)$; two estimators with equal variance are not equally good if one is slower, and a lower-variance estimator can be the worse choice if it is slow enough.

A: uniform (cheap, noisy) B: importance (the win) C: expensive importance

1. Fix the time, not the sample count

Comparing two estimators at the same number of samples is unfair when one sample costs more. Compare them at the same time instead. If an estimator has per-sample variance $V$ and per-sample cost $t$, a budget $T$ buys $n=T/t$ samples, and the variance of the $n$-sample mean is

$$\operatorname{Var}[\hat I_n]=\frac{V}{n}=\frac{V\,t}{T}.$$

For a fixed $T$, the achievable error is proportional to the product $V\,t$ — so that is what you minimize, and the figure of merit is its reciprocal:

$$\boxed{\ \varepsilon=\frac{1}{V\cdot t}\ }$$

Variance and cost enter symmetrically. Halving the variance and doubling the cost is a wash. The only thing that matters is the product — which is why "lower variance" is not, by itself, "better."

2. Three estimators, timed

The file estimates $\int_0^1 e^x\,dx$ three ways, measuring wall-clock time: A uniform sampling (cheap, high variance); B importance sampling (one extra sqrt, ~9× less variance, example 06); and C, the same estimator as B but with a deliberately expensive pdf evaluation (extra transcendental work, standing in for a heavy tabulated or measured pdf). B wins. Its ~9× lower variance costs only ~1.6× more time, so $\varepsilon_B/\varepsilon_A=9/1.6\approx5.5$ — for any fixed time budget it is far ahead. C is the cautionary tale: it has the identical low variance of B, yet its expensive per-sample work makes it $\sim$10× less efficient than even the cheap uniform method. Same variance, opposite verdict — because variance was never the whole story. (Cost is wall-clock and machine-dependent; the relative efficiencies are the durable point.)

4. In the renderer

Efficiency is the lens every choice in this series should be viewed through. Multiple importance sampling (09), the GGX VNDF sampler (19), next-event estimation (24), control variates (27) — each earns its place only when the variance it removes outweighs the cost it adds. It is why renderers prize cheap samplers like the analytic VNDF weight and Schlick's Fresnel (20) over more accurate but slower alternatives, why a closed-form approximation often beats a perfect-but-costly integral, and why profiling a path tracer means watching variance per millisecond, not per sample. A fancy technique that doubles cost to shave a little variance is a regression — measure the product, always.

5. The code, and the throughline

The complete sample on GitHub: 31_efficiency.cpp.

Measure both halves — variance and wall-clock — then score by their product.

// run a sampler N times, returning per-sample variance AND elapsed seconds
auto t0 = clock();
for (long long i = 0; i < N; ++i) { double e = sampler(); welford(e, mean, m2); }
double secs = elapsed(t0), var = m2 / (N - 1);

// efficiency = 1 / (variance * time).  Compare techniques by THIS, not by variance.
double efficiency = 1.0 / (var * secs);
//   A uniform:    high var, cheap        -> baseline
//   B importance: ~9x less var, ~1.6x cost -> ~5.5x efficiency  (the real win)
//   C same var as B but 88x slower         -> 0.1x efficiency   (slower than brute force)
Takeaway. For a fixed time budget the error variance is $V\,t/T$, so the figure of merit is efficiency $\varepsilon=1/(V\,t)$ — minimize variance times cost, never variance alone. Importance sampling's small extra cost buys a big efficiency gain; an equally-low-variance estimator that is too expensive can fall below brute-force uniform sampling. Every variance-reduction technique in this series is worth it only when $V\,t$ goes down — the scoreboard that closes the toolkit.
