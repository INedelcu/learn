Refraction, Snell's law & total internal reflection

22_dielectric_refraction.cpp — when a ray meets smooth glass it splits: some reflects, the rest bends across the boundary. The three facts a glass shader needs — Snell's bend, the Fresnel split, and the critical-angle mirror — built and checked.

A smooth dielectric — glass, water — does two things to a ray at once: a fraction reflects off the surface, and the rest refracts, bending as it crosses into the new medium. Which way it bends is Snell's law; how the energy divides is the Fresnel term from example 20; and going from dense to thin there is a critical angle past which nothing gets through — total internal reflection, the mirror you see on the underside of water. These are exactly the facts the renderer's SampleGlassGGX is built on.

One-sentence summary. Snell's law $n_i\sin\theta_i=n_t\sin\theta_t$ bends the transmitted ray; the Fresnel reflectance $F$ sets the split (a fraction $F$ reflects, $1-F$ refracts); a path tracer picks one branch stochastically — reflect with probability $F$ — and the branch probability cancels the $F$, so the glass weight collapses to the same bounded $G_2/G_1$ as the opaque case; and when $\sin\theta_i>n_t/n_i$ Snell has no solution, giving total internal reflection past the critical angle $\theta_c=\arcsin(n_t/n_i)$.

1. A ray splits at the surface

At the boundary, energy conservation splits the incoming light in two: the Fresnel fraction $F$ reflects (angle of incidence = angle of reflection), and the remaining $1-F$ transmits at the Snell-bent angle $\theta_t$. Drag the angle and flip the direction below. Going into glass the ray always gets through (bending toward the normal); coming out, watch the refracted ray swing flatter and flatter until, past the critical angle, it vanishes entirely and all the light reflects.

2. Snell's law and the refraction vector

Snell's law relates the angles to the indices of refraction:

$$n_i\sin\theta_i=n_t\sin\theta_t.$$

Into a denser medium ($n_t>n_i$) the ray bends toward the normal ($\theta_t<\theta_i$); into a thinner one it bends away. A renderer wants the transmitted direction as a vector, not an angle. With $\eta=n_i/n_t$ and $\cos\theta_i=-\mathbf n\!\cdot\!\mathbf d$ (for $\mathbf d$ pointing into the surface), the unit transmitted direction is

$$\mathbf t=\eta\,\mathbf d+\big(\eta\cos\theta_i-\sqrt{k}\big)\,\mathbf n,\qquad k=1-\eta^2\big(1-\cos^2\theta_i\big)=1-\eta^2\sin^2\theta_i.$$

The tangential part of $\mathbf t$ is $\eta$ times the tangential part of $\mathbf d$ — which is precisely Snell's law in vector form (tangential momentum scaled by $\eta=n_i/n_t$), and $\sqrt k=\cos\theta_t$ is the normal part. The C++ run confirms the bent direction against $n_i\sin\theta_i=n_t\sin\theta_t$ at every angle (air → glass, $n=1.5$):

Even at $70°$ the ray inside the glass is only $38.8°$ off the normal: a dense medium "pulls in" all incoming directions toward a cone (the basis of a diamond's sparkle).

3. Total internal reflection: the critical angle

The square root in the refraction vector is where total internal reflection hides. A real transmitted direction needs $k\ge0$:

$$k=1-\eta^2\sin^2\theta_i\ge0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \sin\theta_i\le\frac{1}{\eta}=\frac{n_t}{n_i}.$$

Going into a denser medium ($n_t>n_i$, so $n_t/n_i>1$) this always holds — no TIR. But going from dense to thin ($n_i>n_t$) the bound $n_t/n_i$ is less than $1$, so there is a cutoff: the critical angle

$$\boxed{\,\theta_c=\arcsin\!\frac{n_t}{n_i}\,}$$

Beyond it, $k<0$, Snell has no solution, and all the light reflects. For glass→air, $\theta_c=\arcsin(1/1.5)=41.8°$ — and the C++ run flips exactly there: it still transmits at $41°$ but is total internal reflection at $42°$. The plot shows the Fresnel curves for both directions; the glass→air curve climbs and then jumps to $F=1$ at the critical angle and stays pinned.

4. Splitting the energy: one branch, and the $G_2/G_1$ weight

A path tracer follows one ray, but a glass hit produces two. The fix is the stochastic pick from example 05: choose the reflect branch with probability $F$ and the refract branch with probability $1-F$. Drawing $40°$ air→glass two million times, the empirical reflect fraction is $0.0460$ against $F=0.0457$ — the split reproduces Fresnel.

Why the glass weight is just $G_2/G_1$. Importance-sampling that discrete choice means dividing each branch's contribution by the probability you took it. The microfacet specular weight (example 19, 20) carries the Fresnel factor of its branch — $F$ for reflection, $1-F$ for transmission — times $G_2/G_1$. Divide by the branch probability and it cancels:

$$\underbrace{\frac{F\cdot G_2/G_1}{F}}_{\text{reflect, prob }F}=\frac{G_2}{G_1},\qquad \underbrace{\frac{(1-F)\cdot G_2/G_1}{1-F}}_{\text{refract, prob }1-F}=\frac{G_2}{G_1}.$$

Either way the per-bounce weight is the same bounded Smith ratio $G_2/G_1$ as the opaque case — no $F$, no spikes. That cancellation is exactly what lets the renderer's SampleGlassGGX reflect or refract with one cheap, low-variance weight.

5. Two subtleties a correct integrator carries

  • Reciprocity. Refraction is invertible (Helmholtz): refract a ray in, then refract the result back across the interface, and you return along the entry direction. The run shows it — in $(0.6428,0,-0.7660)$ refracts in and back out to $(0.6428,0,0.7660)$, the tangential component $0.6428$ recovered exactly — which is what lets light be traced from either the eye or the light.
  • The $n^2$ radiance law. Radiance is not conserved across a refraction: the quantity $L/n^2$ is, so entering a medium of index $n_t$ from $n_i$ scales radiance by $(n_t/n_i)^2$ (Snell compresses the solid angle by the same factor). A glass integrator must multiply by $(n_t/n_i)^2$ on transmission, or it darkens the inside of every refractive object.

6. In the renderer

This is the full skeleton of the PathTracingDemo glass shader. SampleGlassGGX samples a GGX visible normal (example 19), computes the Fresnel $F$ at that microfacet, and stochastically reflects or refracts — total internal reflection falling back to the reflection branch automatically when Snell fails. Because of the cancellation in §4, both branches return the weight $G_2/G_1$. Two details this example sets up are handled there too: the surface push-off (example 19's self-intersection offset) flips sign on the refracted side, since the continuation ray leaves through the back face; and Beer–Lambert absorption $e^{-\sigma t}$ is applied along the transmitted segment for colored glass — the same exponential as the free-flight distance of example 05.

7. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 22_dielectric_refraction.cpp.

The whole of refraction is the vector formula; the $k<0$ test is total internal reflection, and the same $k$ gives the critical angle.

// Refract d (pointing INTO the surface) about normal n, with eta = n_i / n_t.
// Returns false on total internal reflection.
bool Refract(const Vec3& d, const Vec3& n, float eta, Vec3& out) {
    float cosi = -dot(n, d);                          // d points into the surface
    float k = 1.0f - eta*eta * (1.0f - cosi*cosi);    // k = 1 - (eta sin_i)^2
    if (k < 0.0f) return false;                       // TIR: no transmitted ray
    out = eta*d + (eta*cosi - sqrt(k)) * n;           // unit transmitted direction
    return true;
}

// critical angle (dense -> thin):  theta_c = asin(n_t / n_i)
// stochastic split:  reflect with probability F, else refract — the branch
// probability cancels F, leaving the glass weight = G2 / G1 (Fresnel = pick).
Takeaway. At a smooth dielectric a ray reflects and refracts: Snell's law $n_i\sin\theta_i=n_t\sin\theta_t$ bends the transmitted ray (the vector form scales the tangential direction by $\eta=n_i/n_t$), the Fresnel $F$ sets the split, and a path tracer picks one branch with probability $F$ — the probability cancelling $F$ so the weight is the same bounded $G_2/G_1$ as opaque GGX. Past the critical angle $\theta_c=\arcsin(n_t/n_i)$ the square root goes imaginary and all light reflects. With the $(n_t/n_i)^2$ radiance factor and Beer–Lambert absorption, that is the whole of the renderer's glass.
