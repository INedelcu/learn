Equiangular sampling for volumetric single scattering

34_equiangular_sampling.cpp — a camera ray through fog near a bright light gathers in-scattered light that spikes at the closest point. Sample distances by the 1/d² falloff — whose inverse CDF is an arctangent — and the variance collapses.

Picture a camera ray crossing fog near a small bright light — the classic god-rays setup. The in-scattered light gathered along the ray is dominated by the inverse-square falloff to the light, $1/(h^2+t^2)$, where $t$ runs along the ray and $h$ is its closest distance to the light. That integrand is a sharp spike near the closest point, and sampling $t$ uniformly wastes almost every sample far from it. Equiangular sampling (Kulla & Conty 2012) draws $t$ with a density matching $1/(h^2+t^2)$ — which has an analytic inverse CDF — so the estimator is nearly constant and the variance nearly vanishes.

One-sentence summary. The single-scattering integrand spikes as $1/(h^2+t^2)$ near the ray's closest approach to the light; equiangular sampling draws $t$ with density $\propto 1/(h^2+t^2)$ via the arctangent inverse CDF $t=h\tan\!\big(\theta_a+u(\theta_b-\theta_a)\big)$ — so $f/p$ is constant (zero variance for the bare falloff, the importance-sampling ideal of example 06) while uniform sampling sprays its samples where the integrand is nearly zero.

1. The integrand spikes at the closest point

Along the camera ray, the distance to the light at parameter $t$ is $d(t)=\sqrt{h^2+t^2}$, minimized at $t=0$ (the closest point). The in-scattered contribution falls as $1/d^2$:

$$f(t)=\frac{1}{h^2+t^2},$$

a Lorentzian spike of width $\sim h$ centered on $t=0$. The smaller $h$ — the nearer the ray passes the light — the sharper the spike and the more concentrated the energy. Sampling $t$ uniformly over the ray segment puts most samples out on the flat tails where $f\approx0$, and only rarely lands one under the spike that holds nearly all the integral — the recipe for high variance. 2. Sample the angle: an arctangent inverse CDF

Importance-sample the falloff: take $p(t)\propto 1/(h^2+t^2)$. The normalizer over $[a,b]$ is its integral — and $\int 1/(h^2+t^2)\,dt=\tfrac1h\arctan(t/h)$, so

$$Z=\int_a^b\frac{dt}{h^2+t^2}=\frac1h\Big(\arctan\tfrac{b}{h}-\arctan\tfrac{a}{h}\Big),\qquad p(t)=\frac{1}{Z\,(h^2+t^2)}.$$

Invert the CDF (example 05): set the cumulative $\frac1{Zh}\big(\arctan\tfrac th-\arctan\tfrac ah\big)$ equal to $u$ and solve for $t$. The $\arctan$ turns the substitution into a uniform draw in angle — hence "equiangular":

$$\boxed{\,t=h\tan\!\Big(\theta_a+u\,(\theta_b-\theta_a)\Big),\quad \theta_a=\arctan\tfrac ah,\ \theta_b=\arctan\tfrac bh\,}$$

Geometrically, $u$ sweeps the angle subtended at the light uniformly between the segment's endpoints, and $t=h\tan\theta$ maps that back to distance along the ray. Equal angle per sample is exactly the $1/d^2$ density.

3. The variance collapses

Because the density is the integrand up to the constant $Z$, the per-sample estimator is

$$\frac{f(t)}{p(t)}=\frac{1/(h^2+t^2)}{1/\big(Z(h^2+t^2)\big)}=Z\quad\text{(constant!)},$$

so every sample returns the exact integral $Z$ — variance zero. This is the perfect-importance-sampling ideal $p\propto f$ from example 06, here realized exactly because the dominant factor $1/d^2$ has a closed-form inverse CDF. The C++ run integrates $\int_{-1}^{1.5}1/(h^2+t^2)\,dt$ with $h=0.1$ (exact $29.7536$):

4. In the renderer

Equiangular sampling is the standard way to choose the scatter distance for volumetric single scattering — god rays, light shafts, the glow around a street lamp in fog. A real estimator also carries the transmittance $T(t)$ along the ray and the phase function, so the density no longer matches $f$ exactly and the cancellation is no longer perfect — but the $1/d^2$ factor is by far the spikiest part, so equiangular still wins by large margins, and it is routinely combined with a transmittance-based distance sample through MIS (example 24): equiangular owns the near-light spike, the transmittance sample owns the falloff through dense media, and MIS takes the better of the two per sample. It is the volumetric cousin of BRDF importance sampling — match the density to the integrand's dominant factor, and the noise goes away.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 34_equiangular_sampling.cpp.

One tan of a uniform angle gives the distance; the estimator is then exact.

double atanA = std::atan(a / h), atanB = std::atan(b / h);
double Z = (atanB - atanA) / h;                          // integral of 1/(h^2+t^2) over [a,b]

// equiangular: sample t with density proportional to 1/(h^2+t^2)
double t = h * std::tan(atanA + rng.NextFloat() * (atanB - atanA));   // arctan inverse CDF
double p = (1.0 / (h*h + t*t)) / Z;                      // normalized density = f/Z
double est = (1.0 / (h*h + t*t)) / p;                    // f / p = Z  ->  exact, zero variance

// uniform (for contrast): t ~ U[a,b], estimator f(t)*(b-a) -- spikes near t=0, noisy.
Takeaway. Volumetric single scattering near a light is governed by the $1/(h^2+t^2)$ falloff — a sharp spike uniform sampling can't find. Equiangular sampling draws the distance with density $\propto1/(h^2+t^2)$ through the arctangent inverse CDF $t=h\tan(\theta_a+u(\theta_b-\theta_a))$, making $f/p=Z$ constant: zero variance for the bare falloff (the example-06 ideal), and a large win even with transmittance and phase folded in. It is how renderers sample distance for god rays.
