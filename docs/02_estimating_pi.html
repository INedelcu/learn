Estimating π, and the 1/√N convergence law

02_estimating_pi.cpp — the “hello world” of Monte Carlo, and a hands-on look at the single most important fact about it: the error only shrinks like one over the square root of the sample count.

Throw darts uniformly at the unit square and count how many land inside the quarter circle. The fraction inside estimates $\pi/4$, so four times that fraction estimates $\pi$. It is a cute trick, but its real job is to give us a concrete place to watch the law that governs every path-traced image: to halve the noise you need four times the samples.

One-sentence summary. A Monte Carlo estimate is an average of $N$ random samples; the Central Limit Theorem makes its error fall as $\sigma/\sqrt N$ — so the error times $\sqrt N$ settles to a constant (here $\approx1.64$) rather than to zero, and variance-reduction methods can only shrink that constant, never beat the $1/\sqrt N$ rate.

1. The idea: area as a probability

Scatter darts uniformly over the unit square $[0,1]\times[0,1]$. A dart at $(x,y)$ counts as a hit when it lands inside the quarter circle $x^2+y^2\le1$. A full unit circle has area $\pi r^2=\pi$, so this quarter of it has area $\tfrac14\pi$. Because the darts are uniform, the probability of a hit is just the area of the hit region divided by the area of the square:

$$p=\Pr[\text{hit}]=\frac{\text{area of quarter circle}}{\text{area of square}}=\frac{\pi/4}{1}=\frac{\pi}{4}\approx0.7854 .$$

So the observed hit fraction estimates $\pi/4$, and multiplying by four estimates $\pi$ itself.

2. It is really Monte Carlo integration

The dart game is a disguised integral. Define the indicator $f(x,y)=1$ when $x^2+y^2\le1$ and $0$ otherwise. Its expectation over the uniform square is exactly the area we want:

$$\mathbb E[f]=\int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 f(x,y)\,dx\,dy=\frac\pi4 .$$

Just like example 01, we cannot always do such an integral by hand, so we estimate the expectation by averaging samples of $f$. Each dart is one Bernoulli trial $I_i\in\{0,1\}$, and the estimator is the sample mean scaled by four:

$$\hat\pi_N=4\cdot\hat p_N=\frac4N\sum_{i=1}^N I_i .$$

It is unbiased: $\mathbb E[\hat\pi_N]=4\,\mathbb E[I_i]=4p=\pi$. On average it is right; the only question is how fast the scatter around that average shrinks.

3. Throw the darts

Throw a few thousand and watch the estimate jump around $\pi$. Notice it is still wobbling in the second decimal place even after thousands of darts — that sluggishness is the whole story of this page.

4. The 1/√N law — where the slowness comes from

Every Monte Carlo estimate is an average of $N$ independent samples, and two facts about variance fix how fast its scatter shrinks: a constant pulled outside the variance gets squared, $\operatorname{Var}[aX]=a^2\operatorname{Var}[X]$, and for independent terms the variance of a sum is the sum of the variances. Apply both to $\hat p_N=\frac1N\sum_i I_i$:

$$\begin{aligned} \operatorname{Var}[\hat p_N]=\operatorname{Var}\!\Big[\tfrac1N\sum_{i=1}^N I_i\Big] &=\frac1{N^2}\sum_{i=1}^N\operatorname{Var}[I_i] =\frac{N\,\operatorname{Var}[I_i]}{N^2} =\frac{\operatorname{Var}[I_i]}{N},\\ \text{so}\quad \operatorname{SE}[\hat p_N]&=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}[\hat p_N]}=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt N},\qquad \sigma=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}[I_i]}. \end{aligned}$$

The standard error — the typical size of the estimate's error — therefore carries a $1/\sqrt N$, no matter the problem. For our Bernoulli darts each trial is $I_i\in\{0,1\}$, so $I_i^2=I_i$ and the per-sample variance follows from the same $\overline{x^2}-\bar x^{\,2}$ identity used in example 01:

$$\operatorname{Var}[I_i]=\mathbb E[I_i^2]-\mathbb E[I_i]^2=p-p^2=p(1-p).$$

The estimator is $\hat\pi_N=4\hat p_N$, and scaling by $4$ multiplies the standard error by $4$, so

$$\operatorname{SE}[\hat\pi_N]=4\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{N}}=\frac{C}{\sqrt N},\qquad C=4\sqrt{p(1-p)}\approx1.642 .$$

Plugging in the hit probability $p=\pi/4\approx0.785$ gives $p(1-p)\approx0.1686$, so $C=4\sqrt{0.1686}\approx1.642$ — the constant the diagnostics below keep circling.

The brutal arithmetic of $1/\sqrt N$. Because the error scales as $N^{-1/2}$:
  • To halve the error you need 4× the samples.
  • To win one more decimal digit (10× less error) you need 100× the samples.
This is exactly why path-traced images are noisy and why the noise is so stubborn: the noise is the Monte Carlo error, and it only falls off as $1/\sqrt{\text{samples per pixel}}$.

5. Watch the error fall — and track its prediction

Plotting the absolute error against $N$ on log–log axes turns the power law $\text{error}\approx C\,N^{-1/2}$ into a straight line. Take the logarithm of both sides:

$$\log(\text{error})\approx\log C-\tfrac12\log N,$$

a line in $\log N$ with slope $-\tfrac12$ and intercept $\log C$. The measured error (blue) rattles around that predicted line (orange) — same slope, same ballpark — confirming the formula really does describe the noise.

6. error × √N: the constant that refuses to vanish

Here is the file’s sharpest diagnostic. If the error truly behaves like $C/\sqrt N$, then multiplying it by $\sqrt N$ should cancel the $N$-dependence and leave a flat, wobbling constant — not something that keeps falling toward zero. It does:

Why this matters for rendering. That flat constant $C$ is the only thing you can actually improve. Importance sampling, stratification, and MIS (the later examples) all shrink $C$ — sometimes dramatically — but the curve still flattens out at some height and the rate is still $1/\sqrt N$. No unbiased Monte Carlo method beats $1/\sqrt N$; they compete on the constant.

7. The price of precision

Invert the law to see the cost. Set the standard error equal to a target $\varepsilon$ and solve for $N$:

$$\varepsilon=\frac{C}{\sqrt N}\;\Longrightarrow\;\sqrt N=\frac{C}{\varepsilon}\;\Longrightarrow\;N\approx\Big(\frac{C}{\varepsilon}\Big)^2.$$

The square is the whole story: the required sample count grows quadratically as the target error shrinks. Drag $\varepsilon$ below and watch $N$ explode.

8. The program, end to end

The complete sample on GitHub: 02_estimating_pi.cpp.

The C++ file streams 100 million darts, reporting at each decade the estimate, the actual error, the predicted error $C/\sqrt N$, and the diagnostic error$\times\sqrt N$:

Rng rng(/*sequence=*/2);
const double p = M_PI / 4.0;                 // true hit probability
const double C = 4.0 * sqrt(p * (1.0 - p));  // error*sqrt(N) ~ this constant (~1.64)

long long inside = 0, n = 0, nextReport = 100;
for (long long i = 0; i < 100000000; ++i) {
    float x = rng.NextFloat(), y = rng.NextFloat();
    if (x*x + y*y <= 1.0f) ++inside;         // a hit
    ++n;
    if (n == nextReport) {
        double est     = 4.0 * (double)inside / n;
        double err     = fabs(est - M_PI);
        double predErr = C / sqrt((double)n);
        printf("%lld  est=%.6f  err=%.6f  pred=%.6f  err*sqrtN=%.4f\n",
               n, est, err, predErr, err * sqrt((double)n));
        nextReport *= 10;
    }
}

9. Takeaways

  • Geometry → probability → integral. The dart fraction estimates the area $\pi/4$; it is Monte Carlo integration of an indicator function, and the estimator $\hat\pi_N=4\hat p_N$ is unbiased.
  • Error $\propto1/\sqrt N$. Because a sample mean has variance $\sigma^2/N$, the standard error is $\sigma/\sqrt N$ — here $C/\sqrt N$ with $C=4\sqrt{p(1-p)}\approx1.64$.
  • The cost is quadratic. Halving the error costs 4× the samples; one more digit costs 100×.
  • error$\times\sqrt N$ is constant. The clearest evidence of the rate: it flattens, it does not fall.
  • Variance reduction shrinks $C$, not the exponent. Importance sampling, stratification, and MIS (later examples) lower the constant; nothing unbiased beats $1/\sqrt N$. This is the thread that runs through the whole series — and it connects back to the variance you learned to measure in example 01.
