The rendering equation as a Neumann series

32_neumann_series.cpp ŌĆö "outgoing = emitted + reflected" is a fixed-point equation; expanding it gives a sum over bounce orders. A path tracer walks that series, and the bounce-count cap is just where you stop summing.

The rendering equation is deceptively short: outgoing radiance equals what a surface emits plus what it reflects, $L=L_e+TL$, where the transport operator $T$ gathers incoming light over the hemisphere and weights it by the BRDF and cosine ŌĆö one bounce. Because $L$ appears on both sides, it is a fixed-point equation; solving it by repeated substitution unrolls it into the Neumann series ŌĆö a sum over the number of bounces light made before reaching the eye. Direct light, one-bounce GI, two-bounce GI, and so on are just its terms, and a path tracer's bounce-count knob chooses how many you keep.

One-sentence summary. Substituting $L=L_e+TL$ into itself gives $L=\sum_{k\ge0}T^k L_e$ ŌĆö term $k$ is light that bounced exactly $k$ times ŌĆö which converges whenever $T$ contracts (reflectance $<1$); capping a path tracer at $K$ bounces drops every term past $k=K$, losing exactly $\rho^{K+1}/(1-\rho)\cdot E$ of the energy in a diffuse enclosure, and a Monte Carlo path tracer's per-order contributions reproduce the terms $E\rho^k$ that sum to the equilibrium $E/(1-\rho)$.

1. Unrolling the fixed point

The rendering equation is $L=L_e+TL$. The unknown $L$ is on both sides, so substitute the whole right-hand side back in for the $L$ it contains, again and again:

$$L=L_e+TL=L_e+T(L_e+TL)=L_e+TL_e+T^2L=\;\cdots\;=\sum_{k=0}^{K}T^kL_e+\underbrace{T^{K+1}L}_{\text{remainder}}.$$

If $T$ contracts ŌĆö each bounce loses energy, reflectance below 1 ŌĆö then $T^{K+1}L\to0$ and the remainder vanishes, leaving the Neumann series (equivalently $L=(I-T)^{-1}L_e$):

$$\boxed{\,L=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}T^kL_e\,}$$

Each term has a physical meaning: $T^kL_e$ is emitted light that has bounced exactly $k$ times before arriving. $k=0$ is light seen directly from sources; $k=1$ is one-bounce indirect; and so on. "Direct lighting" and "$n$-bounce global illumination" are simply prefixes of this one sum.

2. Each term is a bounce order; truncation drops the tail

To make it concrete, take a diffuse enclosure where every surface emits $E$ and reflects a fraction $\rho$ of what it receives. One bounce of a uniform radiance field is just a cosine-weighted average of a constant (example 08), which multiplies it by $\rho$ ŌĆö so here $T$ acts as "multiply by $\rho$", and $T^kL_e=E\rho^k$. The series is the geometric one from example 10:

$$L=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}E\rho^k=\frac{E}{1-\rho},\qquad\text{and capping at $K$ misses }\sum_{k=K+1}^{\infty}E\rho^k=\frac{\rho^{K+1}}{1-\rho}\,E.$$

That truncation tail is exactly the "too dark" of too few bounces. With $E=1,\rho=0.6$ the equilibrium is $2.5$; stopping at $K=4$ leaves $0.6^5/0.4=0.194$ of the energy on the table. 3. A path tracer walks the series

A real $T$ is an integral operator, estimated stochastically: each path is a random walk that accumulates $E$ at every vertex scaled by its running throughput, then bounces (multiply by $\rho$) and continues by Russian roulette (example 10). Binning each path's contributions by bounce order, the Monte Carlo estimates reproduce the Neumann terms $E\rho^k$ ŌĆö and sum to the equilibrium. 4. In the renderer

The PathTracingDemo ray-gen loop is this series made into code: it traces a path, adding emission * throughput at each hit and multiplying throughput by the albedo per bounce ŌĆö accumulating $T^kL_e$ as it goes. The bounce-count uniforms (g_BounceCountOpaque / g_BounceCountTransparent) are the cap $K$: raise them and you sum more Neumann terms (brighter, more accurate indirect light) at more cost; lower them and the tail $\rho^{K+1}/(1-\rho)$ goes missing as too-dark interreflections. Russian roulette (example 10) is the unbiased way to terminate the sum without a hard cap, and every variance-reduction technique in this series is ultimately about estimating these operator powers $T^k$ more cheaply.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 32_neumann_series.cpp.

The path loop accumulates $E$ at each vertex scaled by throughput, binned by bounce order ŌĆö the Neumann terms, sampled.

double tp = 1.0; int k = 0;                       // throughput, bounce order
for (;;) {
    orderSum[k] += E * tp;                       // contribution of order k  (-> T^k Le)
    if (rng.NextFloat() >= q) break;             // Russian roulette terminate (ex. 10)
    tp *= rho / q;                               // reflect (rho) and RR-compensate (1/q)
    ++k;
}
// orderSum[k] / paths  ->  E * rho^k  (the k-th Neumann term);  sum -> E/(1-rho).
// a real T is an integral operator estimated by the random bounce directions.
Takeaway. The rendering equation $L=L_e+TL$ unrolls into the Neumann series $L=\sum_k T^kL_e$ ŌĆö a sum over bounce orders, convergent because each bounce loses energy. Term $k$ is $k$-bounce light; a path tracer estimates the series by random walks, and its bounce cap $K$ simply truncates it, dropping $\rho^{K+1}/(1-\rho)\,E$ in a diffuse enclosure. Direct lighting, GI, and the bounce-count knob are all one geometric series ŌĆö the structure underneath everything the renderer integrates.
