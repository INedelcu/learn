The Monte Carlo integration estimator

03_monte_carlo_integration.cpp — the one formula behind the whole field: turn a definite integral into the average of random samples, scaled by their probability density.

Examples 01 and 02 estimated specific quantities — a mean, the area $\pi/4$. This file states the general tool they were secretly using. To compute a definite integral $I=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ that has no convenient antiderivative, draw random points, average $f$ over them, and rescale. The estimator never needs to know the closed-form integral — only how to evaluate $f$ — which is exactly why it survives the jump to the high-dimensional integrals of light transport.

One-sentence summary. For samples drawn from any density $p$, the average of $f(X_i)/p(X_i)$ is an unbiased estimate of $\int f$, because the $p$ in the denominator cancels the $p$ in the expectation integral; with the simplest choice — uniform $p=\tfrac1{b-a}$ — this collapses to “(domain width) × (average of $f$).”

1. The goal: an integral we can’t do by hand

We want the definite integral of some function over an interval:

$$I=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx .$$

For a few textbook $f$ there is a tidy antiderivative. For most real integrands — and for the light-transport integrals later in this series — there is none. Monte Carlo sidesteps the antiderivative entirely: it only ever evaluates $f$ at points.

2. The estimator: domain width × average height

Draw $N$ points uniformly on $[a,b]$ and form

$$\hat I_N=(b-a)\cdot\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i),\qquad X_i\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[a,b].$$

Read it geometrically: $\tfrac1N\sum f(X_i)$ is the average height of the curve at random places, and multiplying by the width $(b-a)$ gives the area of a rectangle that has the same area as the region under $f$. The integral is “average height times width”; Monte Carlo just estimates the average height by sampling.

3. Why it works — the $f/p$ identity

The estimator above is one case of a deeper formula. The trick is to write the average not of $f$ but of the ratio $f/p$, where $p$ is the density the samples are drawn from. The uniform density on $[a,b]$ is

$$p(x)=\frac{1}{b-a},$$

and the estimator can be rewritten as the plain average of $f(X_i)/p(X_i)$:

$$\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\frac{f(X_i)}{p(X_i)}=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\frac{f(X_i)}{1/(b-a)}=(b-a)\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i).$$

Now take its expectation. The defining property of a sample drawn from $p$ is that $\mathbb E[g(X)]=\int g(x)\,p(x)\,dx$. Apply it with $g=f/p$:

$$\mathbb E\!\left[\frac{f(X)}{p(X)}\right]=\int_a^b\frac{f(x)}{p(x)}\,p(x)\,dx=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=I.$$
The $p$’s cancel. That single cancellation is the entire theory of Monte Carlo integration. It says: sample from $p$, divide each sample of $f$ by the density $p$ at that point, average — and you get the integral, exactly in expectation, with no bias. The sample mean of $f/p$ is an unbiased estimator of $I$ for any valid $p$.

Because it is an average of $N$ i.i.d. terms, everything from example 01 and 02 carries over: the Law of Large Numbers makes it converge, and its error falls as $1/\sqrt N$ (more in §6). The variance of that average — how noisy the estimate is — depends on how much the ratio $f/p$ varies from sample to sample, which is the hook for the next idea.

4. Any density works — and that freedom is the whole game

The identity in §3 used no special property of the uniform density. It holds for any $p(x)$ that is nonzero everywhere $f$ is nonzero (otherwise you would divide by zero in a region that contributes to $I$). This file uses the simplest $p$ — uniform — but the freedom to choose $p$ is what later examples exploit:

  • If $p$ is roughly shaped like $f$, the ratio $f/p$ is nearly constant, so its variance is tiny and the estimate converges fast. This is importance sampling (example 06).
  • In the extreme, $p\propto f$ makes $f/p$ exactly constant — zero variance, every sample lands on the answer.

That extreme is worth seeing explicitly. The only density proportional to a nonnegative $f$ and integrating to $1$ is $p(x)=f(x)/I$; then the ratio is the same at every sample,

$$\frac{f(X)}{p(X)}=\frac{f(X)}{f(X)/I}=I,$$

a constant, so its variance is exactly $0$ — one sample already returns $I$. You can't actually build this $p$ (it needs $I$, the unknown you're after), but it is the target importance sampling chases: the closer $p$ is to $f/I$, the flatter $f/p$ and the lower the noise.

Below, all three densities estimate the same integral $\int_0^1 e^x\,dx=e-1$. Switch between them: the estimate lands on $1.718$ every time (unbiased, as promised), but the spread of the $f/p$ values — and therefore the noise — collapses as $p$ better matches $f$.

The file integrates two functions with known answers, so the estimate can be checked:

$$\int_0^1 e^x\,dx=e-1\approx1.7182818,\qquad\qquad\int_0^\pi \sin x\,dx=2 .$$

6. Convergence: the same 1/√N as everything else

Because the estimator is a sample mean, its error obeys the law from example 02: absolute error $\propto1/\sqrt N$, a straight line of slope $-\tfrac12$ on log–log axes. Both integrands follow it; only the constant out front differs (it is set by the variance of $f/p$, i.e. by how much each integrand wiggles).

Concretely, the estimator is the sample mean of the i.i.d. values $Y_i=f(X_i)/p(X_i)$, so the variance-of-the-mean result from example 02 applies verbatim:

$$\operatorname{Var}[\hat I_N]=\frac1N\operatorname{Var}\!\left[\frac{f(X)}{p(X)}\right],\qquad \operatorname{SE}[\hat I_N]=\frac{\operatorname{std}(f/p)}{\sqrt N}.$$

The constant multiplying $1/\sqrt N$ is therefore exactly the standard deviation of the $f/p$ values — the very spread you watched collapse in Figure 2. Matching $p$ to $f$ shrinks that standard deviation; nothing changes the $1/\sqrt N$ rate itself.

7. The same formula is the rendering equation

This is not just an analogy. The outgoing radiance at a surface point is a hemispherical integral, and a path tracer estimates it with exactly the $f/p$ formula from §3 — sampling directions $\omega_i$ from some density $p(\omega)$ and averaging the integrand over $p$:

$$L_o(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H^2} f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,L_i(\omega_i)\,\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i\;\approx\;\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\frac{f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,L_i(\omega_i)\,\cos\theta_i}{p(\omega_i)} .$$

8. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 03_monte_carlo_integration.cpp.

The helper is the estimator verbatim. Note the comment on line 48: dividing by the uniform density $p=1/(b-a)$ is the same as multiplying by $(b-a)$, so the $/p$ is “folded into” the trailing $(b-a)$ factor rather than written out.

// estimate the integral of f over [a,b] with N uniform samples
template <typename F>
double EstimateIntegral(F f, double a, double b, long long n, Rng& rng) {
    double sum = 0.0;
    for (long long i = 0; i < n; ++i) {
        double x = rng.NextFloat((float)a, (float)b);  // X_i ~ Uniform[a,b]
        sum += f(x);                 // f(X_i)/p(X_i) with p=1/(b-a), folded
    }                                // into the (b-a) factor below
    return (b - a) * sum / n;        // (b-a) * average of f
}

// ... in main(): both integrals checked at N = 10, 100, ... , 10,000,000
auto fExp = [](double x){ return std::exp(x); };   // exact: e - 1
auto gSin = [](double x){ return std::sin(x); };   // exact: 2
EstimateIntegral(fExp, 0.0, 1.0, n, rng);
EstimateIntegral(gSin, 0.0, kPi, n, rng);

9. Takeaways

  • The estimator. $\hat I_N=\frac1N\sum f(X_i)/p(X_i)$ estimates $\int f$ for samples drawn from density $p$; with uniform $p$ it is “(domain width) × (average of $f$).”
  • Why it’s unbiased. $\mathbb E[f/p]=\int (f/p)\,p\,dx=\int f$ — the sampling density cancels. True for any $p$ that is nonzero wherever $f$ is.
  • Choice of $p$ is the only knob. It does not change what the estimator converges to (always $I$), only how fast — the variance of $f/p$. Matching $p$ to $f$ is importance sampling (example 06).
  • Convergence is $1/\sqrt N$. As a sample mean it inherits the rate and analysis of examples 01–02.
  • No antiderivative required. The estimator only evaluates $f$ at points, so it scales to the high-dimensional, no-closed-form integrals of light transport — where it appears as the rendering equation, an average of $f_r L_i\cos\theta / p(\omega)$.

References — the $f/p$ estimator is the foundation of Monte Carlo integration; see Pharr, Jakob & Humphreys, Physically Based Rendering, §13.1–13.3, and Veach’s thesis, Robust Monte Carlo Methods for Light Transport Simulation (1997), ch. 2. The $1/\sqrt N$ rate is the Central Limit Theorem (example 04); the choice of $p$ is developed in example 06 (importance sampling).
