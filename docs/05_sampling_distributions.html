Sampling a chosen distribution

05_sampling_distributions.cpp — two workhorse recipes, inversion and rejection, for turning a plain Uniform[0,1) draw into a sample from any density $p(x)$ you choose.

Everything so far sampled uniformly. But the entire power of Monte Carlo — the freedom to pick a sampling density $p$ from example 03 — is useless until you can actually draw from a non-uniform $p$. This file gives the two standard ways to do it: inversion (invert the CDF; cheap and exact when you can), and rejection (throw darts under the curve; always works but wastes some). Both consume uniform draws and emit samples distributed as $p$.

One-sentence summary. Inversion: if $U\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$ then $X=P^{-1}(U)$ has density $p$, where $P$ is the CDF — one uniform in, one sample out, no waste. Rejection: bound $p(x)\le M\,q(x)$ with an easy $q$, draw a candidate from $q$, keep it with probability $p(x)/(M\,q(x))$ — completely general, but only a fraction $1/M$ of candidates survive.

1. The goal: from Uniform[0,1) to any density

A probability density $p(x)\ge0$ with $\int p=1$ says how likely each region is. We have a generator of uniform numbers; we want samples whose histogram matches an arbitrary $p$. The trick in both methods is to route the uniformity through $p$ — either by warping the number through the CDF, or by filtering candidates so the survivors are distributed as $p$.

2. Inversion: warp a uniform number through the CDF

Given $p(x)$, its cumulative distribution function (CDF) accumulates probability mass from the left and rises from $0$ to $1$:

$$P(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x}p(t)\,dt .$$

The claim is short and powerful:

$$\boxed{\,U\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]\ \Longrightarrow\ X=P^{-1}(U)\ \text{has density }p.\,}$$

Why. $P(x)$ is exactly “the fraction of mass at or below $x$.” Picking $U$ uniformly chooses a mass fraction at random; $P^{-1}$ then asks “which $x$ has that much mass below it?” Where $p$ is large the CDF climbs steeply, so a wide band of $U$ values funnels into a narrow band of $x$ — landing more samples there, exactly as $p$ demands.

Worked example A — $p(x)=2x$ on $[0,1]$

A linearly-rising density. Integrate: $P(x)=\int_0^x 2t\,dt=x^2$. Invert: $P^{-1}(u)=\sqrt u$. So a single line samples it:

$$X=\sqrt{U}.$$

Larger $U$ stretches toward $1$ under the square root’s flattening — piling samples toward the right, where $p=2x$ is largest. (Try the p(x)=2x button above.)

Worked example B — the exponential, $p(t)=\lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ on $[0,\infty)$

The CDF is $P(t)=1-e^{-\lambda t}$, which inverts to

$$T=-\frac{\ln(1-U)}{\lambda}.$$

Its mean is $1/\lambda$. This one is everywhere in rendering: it samples the free-flight distance a photon travels before scattering or being absorbed in a participating medium — the Beer–Lambert law, with $\lambda$ the extinction coefficient.

Rendering link. The path tracer’s glass shader applies Beer–Lambert absorption $e^{-\,\text{extinction}\cdot t}$ along the in-glass segment; the exponential sampled here is the same law, used to pick where an interaction happens inside a volume. Inversion gives that distance from one uniform draw, exactly.

3. Rejection: throw darts under the curve

When the CDF has no closed-form inverse, inversion stalls — but you can still sample $p$ as long as you can (a) evaluate $p(x)$ and (b) find a constant $M$ and an easy-to-sample density $q$ with

$$p(x)\le M\,q(x)\quad\text{for all }x.$$

The recipe: draw a candidate $x$ from $q$, then accept it with probability $p(x)/(M\,q(x))$; otherwise discard and try again. Accepted samples follow $p$ exactly. Geometrically, with $q$ uniform it is pure darts: scatter points uniformly under the bounding box of height $M$, keep the ones that fall under the curve $p$.

The cost. Only a fraction $1/M$ of candidates are accepted (the area under $p$ divided by the box area). A loose bound $M$ means most darts are thrown away. That waste is precisely why renderers prefer inversion / importance sampling whenever a closed form exists — and why example 06 is about choosing good densities rather than brute-forcing them.

Worked example C — $p(x)=3x^2$ on $[0,1]$

A valid density ($\int_0^1 3x^2\,dx=1$). With proposal $q=\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$ and $\max_{[0,1]}3x^2=3$, take $M=3$. The acceptance probability simplifies neatly:

$$\frac{p(x)}{M\,q(x)}=\frac{3x^2}{3\cdot1}=x^2,$$

so you accept a uniform candidate $x$ with probability $x^2$. The acceptance rate is $1/M=1/3$: about two of every three candidates are thrown away.

use it whena closed form exists — most rendering pdfs (cosine, GGX, exponential)no closed form, or just prototyping

In practice the renderer leans almost entirely on inversion-style sampling, because the densities it cares about (cosine-weighted hemisphere, GGX/VNDF, the exponential above) have closed-form inverses worked out — see examples 08 and 19. Rejection is the safety net for everything else.

5. Verifying a sampler

How do you know a sampler is correct? Histogram its output and compare each bar to the density it should have. The file’s CheckHistogram does exactly that: empirical density in a bin is $\dfrac{\text{count}}{N\cdot\text{binwidth}}$, compared against $p$ evaluated across the bin. Both interactive figures above are this same test, live — the bars settle onto the $p(x)$ curve as samples accumulate.

6. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 05_sampling_distributions.cpp.

Each sampler is a single expression (inversion) or a short accept/reject loop, followed by the histogram check:

// A — inversion of p(x) = 2x:   X = sqrt(U)
for (int i = 0; i < kN; ++i) buf[i] = std::sqrt(rng.NextFloat());

// B — inversion of the exponential:  T = -ln(1-U)/lambda
float t = -std::log(1.0f - rng.NextFloat()) / lambda;   // mean 1/lambda

// C — rejection for p(x) = 3x^2, proposal Uniform, M = 3
while (got < kN) {
    float x = rng.NextFloat();                  // candidate from q
    ++tries;
    if (rng.NextFloat() < x * x) buf[got++] = x;  // accept w.p. p/(Mq) = x^2
}
// acceptance rate = kN / tries  ->  ~1/3

The reported acceptance rate lands near $1/3$, the exponential’s sample mean near $1/\lambda$, and every measured histogram bar tracks its predicted density — the samplers are correct.

7. Takeaways

  • Inversion. $X=P^{-1}(U)$ turns one uniform draw into one exact sample of $p$. Use it whenever the CDF inverts in closed form. Examples: $p=2x\Rightarrow\sqrt U$; exponential $\Rightarrow-\ln(1-U)/\lambda$.
  • Rejection. Bound $p\le Mq$, draw from $q$, accept with probability $p/(Mq)$. Completely general but accepts only $1/M$ of candidates.
  • The exponential is special — it samples free-flight distance / Beer–Lambert absorption, used directly in volume and glass rendering.
  • Tradeoff. Inversion is cheap, exact, and waste-free but needs a closed form; rejection is universal but pays in thrown-away candidates. Rendering prefers inversion, falling back to rejection.
  • Always verify a sampler by histogramming against the target density.
  • This unlocks example 06: now that we can sample any $p$, we can choose a clever one to slash variance — importance sampling.
