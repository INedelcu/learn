Seeing sample patterns: random vs stratified vs Halton

16_sampling_patterns_2d.cpp — examples 07 and 11 argued with numbers that well-spread samples integrate better. This one shows the same thing with pictures, then lines the pictures up against the error table.

Monte Carlo error is, at bottom, about where the samples land. Independent random points clump together and leave gaps; the clumps are redundant work and the gaps are unsampled regions — together they are the variance. Two cures from earlier in the series fix this by construction: stratification (example 07) puts one jittered point in each grid cell, and low-discrepancy sequences (example 11) lay points down so evenly that no clump ever forms. Here we scatter 256 points all three ways and watch the difference — then confirm it in the integration error.

One-sentence summary. Random samples clump (some grid cells get 3–4 points, a third get none); stratified guarantees exactly one per cell; Halton stays globally even at every prefix — and on the smooth test integral $\iint_{[0,1]^2} e^{x+y}$ that visual difference shows up directly as convergence rate: random error falls as $1/\sqrt N$ while stratified and Halton fall close to $1/N$.

1. Three ways to place 256 points

Each panel below drops $N$ points into the unit square over a $\sqrt N\times\sqrt N$ grid. Random draws each coordinate independently. Stratified places one point at a jittered position inside each cell, $x_i=(i+\xi)/\sqrt N$. Halton is deterministic: the $i$-th point is $\big(\Phi_2(i),\,\Phi_3(i)\big)$, the radical inverses of $i$ in bases 2 and 3. Re-roll to redraw the randomized two (Halton never changes), and watch the cell-occupancy numbers underneath.

2. Why clumps are variance

Drop $N$ points randomly into $N$ cells and the count in each cell is roughly Poisson with mean 1: about 37% of cells end up empty, and a comparable number get two, three, or more. The empty cells are regions the estimator never looks at; the doubled-up cells are samples that mostly repeat information a neighbour already gave. Both inflate the variance. Stratification removes the failure outright — one sample per cell, so no cell is ever empty or crowded — and a low-discrepancy sequence achieves nearly the same evenness deterministically, and it stays even at every prefix length, not only at the full count. The occupancy numbers under Figure 1 are this paragraph made measurable.

Where the 37% comes from. Each of the $N$ points lands in a given cell independently with probability $1/N$, so the chance a particular cell stays empty is

$$\Big(1-\tfrac1N\Big)^{N}\ \xrightarrow[\,N\to\infty\,]{}\ \frac1e\approx0.368,$$

and by linearity of expectation that is also the expected fraction of empty cells — already $0.367$ at $N=256$. More generally the per-cell count converges to a Poisson$(1)$ law, $P(k)=e^{-1}/k!$: roughly $37\%$ empty, $37\%$ with exactly one, $26\%$ with two or more. The crowded and starved cells are the clumps and gaps — the variance — that stratification (one per cell, always) and low-discrepancy sequences engineer away.

4. Stratified wins here — but Halton wins where it counts

On this 2-D integrand jittered stratification actually edges out Halton. So why do production renderers reach for low-discrepancy sequences? Two reasons stratification can't match:

  • It is incremental. A stratified grid needs its sample count fixed up front (you must know $\sqrt N$ to build the cells). Halton lets you keep adding samples forever — the sequence stays low-discrepancy at every prefix — which is exactly what a progressively-refined render wants.
  • It survives high dimensions. A path sample is not 2-D: pixel $(x,y)$, lens $(u,v)$, a light pick, then two random numbers per bounce. A full stratified grid over $d$ dimensions needs $N^{d}$ cells — impossible past a few dimensions (the curse of dimensionality). Low-discrepancy sequences keep spreading well in dozens of dimensions, which is why pbrt's Halton and Sobol samplers exist.

The usual production compromise is to stratify or pad-sample the few dimensions that matter most (pixel, lens, first bounce) and rely on a good sequence for the rest.

5. In the renderer

Every pixel spends its samples on a multi-dimensional unit hypercube, and spreading those samples evenly is free variance reduction — no extra rays, just better-placed ones. The PathTracingDemo renderer keeps it simple: it draws every random number from a per-path Wang-hash RNG (WangHash / RandomFloat01 in Utils.hlsl), seeded per pixel — i.e. the independent-random column of the table above, clumps and all. Replacing that with a stratified pixel jitter or a Halton/Sobol sampler is precisely the kind of upgrade this example argues for: the same sample budget, lower noise, just by refusing to let samples clump.

6. The code

The three generators are a few lines each; RadicalInverse is example 11's, reused for the Halton coordinates.

static double RadicalInverse(unsigned base, uint64_t a) {
    const double invBase = 1.0 / base; double invBaseN = 1.0, r = 0.0;
    while (a) { r += (a % base) * (invBaseN *= invBase); a /= base; }
    return r;
}

const int N = 256, s = 16;                      // 16 x 16 grid for stratified
for (int i = 0; i < N; ++i)                      // RANDOM: independent uniform
    rnd.push_back({rng.NextFloat(), rng.NextFloat()});
for (int j = 0; j < s; ++j) for (int i = 0; i < s; ++i)   // STRATIFIED: one per cell
    strat.push_back({(i + rng.NextFloat()) / s, (j + rng.NextFloat()) / s});
for (int i = 0; i < N; ++i)                      // HALTON: radical inverse, bases 2 & 3
    halt.push_back({(float)RadicalInverse(2, i), (float)RadicalInverse(3, i)});
Takeaway. The variance you fight in Monte Carlo is visible: it is the clumps and gaps of independent random points. Stratification and low-discrepancy sequences place samples so those clumps never form, turning a $1/\sqrt N$ method into a near-$1/N$ one for free. Stratification is simplest in low dimensions; low-discrepancy sequences are what keep that evenness in the high-dimensional hypercube a path tracer actually samples.
