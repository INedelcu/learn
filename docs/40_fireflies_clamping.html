Fireflies: heavy tails and the clamping tradeoff

40_fireflies_clamping.cpp — those lone bright pixels in a noisy render aren't a bug; they're the estimator's heavy tail showing through. When a rare path spikes f/p enormously the variance can be infinite — the mean is right, but nothing looks converged.

The stray white pixels that pock a noisy render — fireflies — are the Monte Carlo estimator's heavy tail made visible. When a rare path carries a huge contribution (a tiny bright light hit through a low-probability direction, a near-singular BSDF/pdf ratio), the estimator $f(X)/p(X)$ spikes enormously. If the tail is heavy enough the estimator has infinite variance: the mean is still correct, but no practical number of samples looks converged, because one giant sample dominates the average. The usual fix, clamping, trades that variance for a darkening bias — the pathology and its cure, both quantifiable.

One-sentence summary. Estimating $\int_0^1 1\,dx=1$ by drawing $X\sim p(x)=2x$ gives the estimator $Y=1/(2X)$ whose mean is $1$ but whose second moment $\int_0^1\frac{1}{2x}dx$ diverges — a power-law tail $P(Y>y)=1/(4y^2)$, so infinite variance and fireflies; clamping at $c$ makes the variance finite but discards the tail energy, biasing the mean low by exactly $-1/(4c)$ — clamp tight for a clean-but-dark image, loose for correct brightness but fireflies.

1. A firefly is the estimator's heavy tail

Build the pathology on purpose. To estimate $I=\int_0^1 1\,dx=1$, draw $X$ from the density $p(x)=2x$ (so $X=\sqrt u$) — a pdf that is tiny near $0$ where the integrand is not. The estimator is

$$Y=\frac{f(X)}{p(X)}=\frac{1}{2X},$$

which blows up as $X\to0$. It is perfectly unbiased — $\mathbb E[Y]=\int_0^1\frac{1}{2x}\cdot 2x\,dx=\int_0^1 1\,dx=1$ — but a rare draw of $X$ near $0$ produces a colossal $Y$. Sample it and watch: the mean sits at $1$, yet the single largest sample is in the thousands, and the sample variance refuses to settle.

2. Why the variance is infinite

The mean exists; the second moment does not. Computing it against the sampling density $p(x)=2x$:

$$\mathbb E[Y^2]=\int_0^1\Big(\frac{1}{2x}\Big)^2\,2x\,dx=\int_0^1\frac{1}{2x}\,dx=\Big[\tfrac12\ln x\Big]_0^1\to+\infty,$$

because $\ln x\to-\infty$ as $x\to0$. So $\operatorname{Var}[Y]=\mathbb E[Y^2]-1=\infty$. Equivalently, the tail is a power law right on the boundary: $P(Y>y)=P\!\big(X<\tfrac1{2y}\big)=\big(\tfrac1{2y}\big)^2=\dfrac{1}{4y^2}$, i.e. the density falls as $y^{-3}$. A $y^{-3}$ density has a finite mean (barely: $\int y\cdot y^{-3}$ converges) but an infinite second moment ($\int y^2\cdot y^{-3}=\int y^{-1}$ diverges) — the exact edge case. The Central Limit Theorem doesn't even apply; the sample mean converges, but slowly and erratically, jerked by each new record sample. In a render, that record sample is the firefly.

3. Clamping: trade variance for a darkening bias

The common fix is to cap each sample at some value $c$: use $\min(Y,c)$. That makes the variance finite, but it throws away the rare-but-real energy in the tail, biasing the mean low. The bias has a clean closed form — subtract the discarded excess:

$$\mathbb E[\min(Y,c)]=1-\int_{0}^{1/(2c)}\!\Big(\tfrac{1}{2x}-c\Big)2x\,dx=1-\Big[x-cx^2\Big]_0^{1/(2c)}=1-\frac{1}{4c},$$

so the clamp bias is exactly $-\dfrac{1}{4c}$, and the (now finite) variance grows only logarithmically, $\operatorname{Var}[\min(Y,c)]=\tfrac12\ln(2c)+\tfrac14-\big(1-\tfrac1{4c}\big)^2$. The C++ table confirms both to the digit:

4. In the renderer

Fireflies show up wherever a path can hit a bright source through a low-probability direction: a small light seen only by a BSDF-sampled ray, a caustic, a sharp specular bounce into the sun. The estimator is unbiased, so they are real energy — averaging removes them eventually, but painfully slowly. Renderers use two remedies. First, sample better so the tail never forms: next-event estimation and light/BSDF MIS (example 24) give the small light a proper pdf, keeping $f/p$ bounded (the whole point of the $G_2/G_1$ and MIS weights throughout this series). Second, when a tail is unavoidable, clamp indirect contributions at some $c$ — a knowingly-biased firefly fix that trades a little darkening for a lot of stability, exactly the $-1/(4c)$-for-variance trade above. The honest move is to clamp only the indirect bounces (where the eye is forgiving) and to make $c$ as loose as the noise budget allows.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 40_fireflies_clamping.cpp.

The heavy-tailed estimator, and the one-line clamp that tames it (at a price).

double X = std::sqrt(rng.NextFloat());       // X ~ p(x) = 2x  (tiny near 0)
double Y = 1.0 / (2.0 * X);                  // estimator f/p, blows up as X -> 0
// E[Y] = 1 (unbiased), but E[Y^2] = integral 1/(2x) dx = INFINITE -> fireflies

double Yc = std::fmin(Y, c);                 // clamp: finite variance, but...
// E[Yc] = 1 - 1/(4c)   -> a darkening bias of exactly -1/(4c)
// Var[Yc] = 0.5*ln(2c) + 0.25 - (1 - 1/(4c))^2   -> grows only as ln(c)
Takeaway. Fireflies are an estimator with a heavy tail: here $Y=1/(2X)$ has mean $1$ but $\mathbb E[Y^2]=\int\frac1{2x}=\infty$, a $y^{-2}$ tail that makes the variance infinite and the sample mean lurch on each record draw. Clamping at $c$ restores finite variance but discards tail energy, biasing the result low by exactly $-1/(4c)$ while the variance grows as $\tfrac12\ln c$ — no $c$ wins on both. The real cure is to sample so the tail never forms (NEE + MIS); clamping is the pragmatic, knowingly-biased backstop.
