Sampling the normal distribution (Box–Muller)

12_gaussian_box_muller.cpp — the Gaussian's CDF has no elementary inverse, so inversion fails; Box–Muller sidesteps it by working in 2-D, turning two uniforms into two independent standard normals.

The normal (Gaussian) is everywhere: it is the limiting shape the Central Limit Theorem produced for our estimator in example 04, and Gaussian kernels are standard reconstruction/blur filters. So we want to sample one. But its CDF is the error function $\operatorname{erf}$, which has no elementary inverse — the inversion method of example 05 can't be applied directly. Box–Muller gets around that with a clever change to polar coordinates.

One-sentence summary. Two independent standard normals form a rotationally-symmetric cloud in the plane, so its angle is uniform and its squared radius is Exponential — both easy to sample by inversion; Box–Muller draws those polar coordinates from two uniforms and converts back: $R=\sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$, $\theta=2\pi U_2$, then $Z_0=R\cos\theta$, $Z_1=R\sin\theta$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal N(0,1)$.

1. Why inversion fails here

The standard normal density is $\varphi(x)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$, and its CDF $\Phi(x)=\tfrac12\big(1+\operatorname{erf}(x/\sqrt2)\big)$. Inversion sampling (example 05) needs $\Phi^{-1}$ in closed form — but $\operatorname{erf}$ has no elementary inverse. So we need a different trick. The trick is to stop thinking in 1-D and sample two normals at once, geometrically.

2. The Box–Muller transform

Take two independent uniforms $U_1,U_2$ and produce two independent standard normals:

$$R=\sqrt{-2\ln U_1},\qquad \theta=2\pi U_2,\qquad \boxed{\,Z_0=R\cos\theta,\quad Z_1=R\sin\theta\,}$$

Then $Z_0,Z_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1)$, independent. For a general $\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)$, shift and scale: $X=\mu+\sigma Z$.

3. Why it works: think in 2-D

Place two independent standard normals as a point $(Z_0,Z_1)$ in the plane. Their joint density multiplies:

$$\varphi(z_0)\,\varphi(z_1)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\,e^{-(z_0^2+z_1^2)/2}=\frac{1}{2\pi}\,e^{-r^2/2},$$

which depends only on the radius $r^2=z_0^2+z_1^2$ — the cloud is rotationally symmetric. In polar coordinates that means:

  • the angle $\theta$ is uniform on $[0,2\pi)$ — no direction is special;
  • the squared radius $S=R^2$ is Exponential with mean 2 (the area element $r\,dr$ turns $e^{-r^2/2}$ into the exponential density $\tfrac12 e^{-s/2}$).
Why — the Jacobian. Change the joint density from Cartesian to polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$. The area element carries a factor $r$ — $dz_0\,dz_1=r\,dr\,d\theta$ — so the density in $(r,\theta)$ is

$$p(r,\theta)=\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-r^2/2}\cdot r=\underbrace{\frac{1}{2\pi}}_{\text{angle}}\cdot\underbrace{r\,e^{-r^2/2}}_{\text{radius}},\qquad r\ge0,\ \theta\in[0,2\pi).$$

It factorizes, so $\theta$ and $R$ are independent: $\theta$ is uniform on $[0,2\pi)$ (constant density $\tfrac{1}{2\pi}$), and $R$ carries the Rayleigh density $r\,e^{-r^2/2}$. Switch to the squared radius $S=R^2$, where $dS=2r\,dr$:

$$p_S(s)=r\,e^{-r^2/2}\,\frac{dr}{dS}=r\,e^{-s/2}\cdot\frac{1}{2r}=\tfrac12 e^{-s/2},$$

exactly the Exponential density with mean $2$. Its CDF is $1-e^{-s/2}$; inverting it (set $1-e^{-S/2}=1-U_1$) gives $S=-2\ln U_1$, i.e. $R=\sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$ — the radius line of the transform, with the angle line $\theta=2\pi U_2$.

Both are samplable by inversion: a uniform angle is just $2\pi U_2$, and the Exponential inverse-CDF gives $S=-2\ln U_1$, so $R=\sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$ (the same exponential trick as example 05). Convert the polar sample $(R,\theta)$ back to Cartesian and you have your two normals.

4. Verify it really is normal

Three checks, the same ones the C++ file runs. Sample many standard normals and compare to the bell: the histogram should trace $\varphi(x)$, the mean should be $0$ and the variance $1$, and the fraction of samples within $1,2,3$ standard deviations should hit the “68–95–99.7” values.

6. Practical notes

  • Avoid $\ln 0$. $U_1$ must be in $(0,1]$, never $0$ — the file draws it as 1 - NextFloat() (since NextFloat() returns $[0,1)$), so $\ln U_1$ is always finite.
  • Trig-free alternative. Marsaglia’s polar method samples a point in the unit disk by rejection (example 05) and reuses its squared radius, avoiding the $\cos/\sin$ — handy when trig is costlier than a few rejected samples.
  • Two at a time. Box–Muller naturally yields a pair; implementations often cache $Z_1$ and return it on the next call.

7. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 12_gaussian_box_muller.cpp.

const double TWO_PI = 6.283185307179586;
for (long long i = 0; i < kPairs; ++i) {
    double u1 = 1.0 - rng.NextFloat();   // in (0,1], so ln(u1) is finite
    double u2 = rng.NextFloat();
    double r  = sqrt(-2.0 * log(u1));
    tally( r * cos(TWO_PI * u2) );       // Z0 ~ N(0,1)
    tally( r * sin(TWO_PI * u2) );       // Z1 ~ N(0,1)
}
// general normal:  X = mu + sigma * Z

The histogram traces $\varphi(x)$ to the third decimal per bin; the moments hit $0$ and $1$, the three band fractions land on the textbook $68.27/95.45/99.73\%$, and the shifted–scaled stream reproduces $\mu=2,\ \sigma=1.5$ — three independent confirmations the output really is $\mathcal N(0,1)$.

This is the same bell the Central Limit Theorem drew for the Monte Carlo estimator itself in example 04 — the per-pixel estimate is approximately $\mathcal N(I,\sigma^2/N)$, which is exactly why $\pm1.96\,\mathrm{SE}$ is a 95% error band. Separately, Gaussian kernels are a common choice of reconstruction filter (weighting sub-pixel samples) and of denoiser blur, so sampling/evaluating a Gaussian is a routine renderer task.

9. Takeaways

  • Inversion can’t sample the normal directly — its CDF $\Phi$ has no elementary inverse.
  • Box–Muller turns two uniforms into two independent standard normals via polar coordinates: $R=\sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$, $\theta=2\pi U_2$.
  • Why it works: a 2-D pair of normals is rotationally symmetric ⇒ uniform angle and Exponential squared-radius, both samplable by inversion.
  • General normals follow by $X=\mu+\sigma Z$.
  • Verified three ways — histogram vs $\varphi$, moments $0$ and $1$, and the 68–95–99.7 rule — the same bell the CLT (example 04) gives the estimator.
