Building an orthonormal basis around a normal

26_orthonormal_basis.cpp — every hemisphere and BRDF sampler works in a local frame where the normal is straight up. To use the directions it produces, you rotate them onto the real normal — which needs two tangents, built stably for any normal (Duff et al. 2017).

The samplers in this series all produce directions in a local frame where the surface normal is $(0,0,1)$: cosine-weighted hemispheres (example 08), GGX visible normals (example 19). A real surface normal points some arbitrary way, so each sampled direction must be rotated from that local frame into world space — which means completing the normal into an orthonormal frame with two tangent vectors. The naive way to find them breaks at the poles; the renderer uses a branchless construction (Duff et al. 2017) that is stable for every normal.

One-sentence summary. A sample $(\ell_x,\ell_y,\ell_z)$ in the local frame becomes the world direction $\ell_x\,\mathbf b_1+\ell_y\,\mathbf b_2+\ell_z\,\mathbf n$, so you need tangents $\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2$ with $(\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2,\mathbf n)$ orthonormal; the Duff–Burgess–Licea-Kane–Theobald sign trick builds them with no branches and no pole blow-up, and because the resulting map is a pure rotation it carries the local distribution onto $\mathbf n$ unchanged — a cosine-weighted lobe stays cosine-weighted, mean $\cos\theta=\tfrac23$.

1. Why a local frame needs a basis

A cosine or GGX sampler returns a direction relative to "up" — the surface normal at $(0,0,1)$ — because the math (the $\cos\theta$, the lobe) is written around that axis. To trace the ray you need it in world space, aligned with the actual normal $\mathbf n$. Given two unit tangents $\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2$ perpendicular to $\mathbf n$ and to each other, the rotation is a single linear combination:

$$\boldsymbol\omega_{\text{world}}=\ell_x\,\mathbf b_1+\ell_y\,\mathbf b_2+\ell_z\,\mathbf n,$$

where $(\ell_x,\ell_y,\ell_z)$ is the local sample. The columns of this map are $(\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2,\mathbf n)$; for it to be a proper rotation — preserving lengths, angles, and the hemisphere — those three must form an orthonormal, right-handed frame. So the whole task reduces to: given $\mathbf n$, find two good tangents.

2. The naive trap and the Duff sign trick

The obvious recipe is $\mathbf b_1=\widehat{\mathbf n\times\hat z}$, $\mathbf b_2=\mathbf n\times\mathbf b_1$. It works — until $\mathbf n$ nears $\pm\hat z$, where $\mathbf n\times\hat z\to\mathbf 0$ and the normalization divides by almost nothing: the frame spins wildly and then explodes. You can patch it with an if that switches the reference axis, but branches are unwelcome on a GPU. Duff et al. (2017) give a branchless construction that never degenerates, using the sign of $n_z$:

$$s=\operatorname{sign}(n_z),\quad a=\frac{-1}{s+n_z},\quad b=n_x n_y a,$$ $$\mathbf b_1=\big(1+s\,n_x^2\,a,\;\; s\,b,\;\; -s\,n_x\big),\qquad \mathbf b_2=\big(b,\;\; s+n_y^2\,a,\;\; -n_y\big).$$

The sign flips the construction between the two hemispheres so the denominator $s+n_z$ is never near zero — at the north pole $s+n_z=2$, at the south pole the sign makes it $-2$, never $0$. Drag the normal below (including straight to a pole) and watch the frame stay rigid and orthonormal:

3. Why it's orthonormal — and stable

The construction looks like magic, but $\mathbf b_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=0$ falls right out. Take the north hemisphere, $s=+1$ so $a=-1/(1+n_z)$, and use $n_x^2+n_y^2=1-n_z^2$:

$$\mathbf b_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=n_x(1+n_x^2 a)+n_y(n_x n_y a)+(-n_x)n_z =n_x\Big[1+a\,(n_x^2+n_y^2)-n_z\Big]=n_x\Big[1+a(1-n_z^2)-n_z\Big].$$

Now $a(1-n_z^2)=-\dfrac{(1-n_z)(1+n_z)}{1+n_z}=-(1-n_z)$, so the bracket is $1-(1-n_z)-n_z=0$, hence $\mathbf b_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=0$. The south hemisphere is identical with $s=-1$ — and that sign is exactly what keeps $1/(s+n_z)$ away from its singularity, so the algebra above never divides by something tiny. The same kind of cancellation gives $\mathbf b_2\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=0$ and $\mathbf b_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf b_2=0$, with $|\mathbf b_1|=|\mathbf b_2|=1$; the C++ check confirms all of it at the floating-point floor ($2.9\times10^{-7}$) over 200k random normals plus both poles.

4. Carrying a distribution onto the normal

Because $(\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2,\mathbf n)$ is orthonormal, the map $\boldsymbol\omega=\ell_x\mathbf b_1+\ell_y\mathbf b_2+\ell_z\mathbf n$ is a pure rotation — its Jacobian (example 23) is exactly $1$. A rotation preserves solid angle, so a distribution sampled in the local frame is carried onto $\mathbf n$ unchanged: a cosine-weighted lobe about $(0,0,1)$ becomes a cosine-weighted lobe about $\mathbf n$. The check is the cosine-weighted average from example 08,

$$\mathbb E[\cos\theta]=\int_{\mathcal H}\cos\theta\,\frac{\cos\theta}{\pi}\,d\omega =\frac1\pi\int_{\mathcal H}\cos^2\theta\,d\omega=\frac1\pi\cdot\frac{2\pi}{3}=\frac23,$$

which must hold about $\mathbf n$ after the rotation. Generating cosine-weighted samples in the local frame and rotating them onto a slanted $\mathbf n$, the C++ run gets mean $\cos\theta=0.66682$ (theory $0.66667$) with zero samples below the surface. 5. In the renderer

This is the small but essential plumbing under every direction sample the renderer takes. PathTracingDemo's BuildOrthonormalBasis is this exact Duff construction; the closest-hit shaders call it to turn a cosine-weighted diffuse bounce or a GGX visible-normal sample — both produced in the local $(0,0,1)$ frame — into a world-space ray around the shading normal. Branchless and pole-stable matters because a renderer evaluates it at every bounce of every path, on the GPU, for normals pointing in every direction; a frame that wobbled near the poles would streak artifacts across exactly the grazing silhouettes where they are most visible.

6. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 26_orthonormal_basis.cpp.

Five lines, no branches, stable everywhere — then a sample is rotated with one linear combination.

// Duff et al. 2017 — branchless orthonormal basis. n must be unit length.
void OrthonormalBasis(const Vec3& n, Vec3& b1, Vec3& b2) {
    float sign = std::copysignf(1.0f, n.z);          // +/-1, never 0
    float a = -1.0f / (sign + n.z);                  // denominator never near 0
    float b = n.x * n.y * a;
    b1 = Vec3(1.0f + sign*n.x*n.x*a,  sign*b,           -sign*n.x);
    b2 = Vec3(b,                       sign + n.y*n.y*a, -n.y);
}

// use it: a local sample (ell) becomes a world direction around n.
Vec3 world = b1*local.x + b2*local.y + n*local.z;    // pure rotation, Jacobian 1
Takeaway. Every hemisphere/BRDF sampler works in a local frame with the normal at $(0,0,1)$; using its output means rotating onto the real normal, $\boldsymbol\omega=\ell_x\mathbf b_1+\ell_y\mathbf b_2+\ell_z\mathbf n$, which needs an orthonormal $(\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2,\mathbf n)$. The Duff sign trick builds the tangents with no branches and no pole blow-up — $\mathbf b_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=0$ drops out of $n_x^2+n_y^2=1-n_z^2$ — and since the map is a pure rotation it carries the sampling distribution onto $\mathbf n$ untouched (cosine stays cosine, mean $\cos\theta=\tfrac23$). It is the transform under every bounce the renderer traces.
