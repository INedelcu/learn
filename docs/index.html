Interactive companion pages for the C++ Monte Carlo tutorial series.

Read them in order; each builds on the last.

The whole series is, ultimately, about one formula — estimating an integral as the average of (integrand)/(sampling density) — and about understanding and shrinking its error. These explainers develop that story one idea at a time.

The Monte Carlo integration estimator

The general estimator: average $f(X_i)/p(X_i)$ for samples drawn from any density $p$. Derives why it is unbiased (the densities cancel), why the choice of $p$ is the only lever on noise, and how the same formula becomes the rendering equation.

Sampling a chosen distribution

Two recipes for turning a Uniform[0,1) draw into a sample from any density $p(x)$: inversion (invert the CDF — exact and waste-free, e.g. $\sqrt U$ and the exponential's $-\ln(1-U)/\lambda$) and rejection (throw darts under the curve — universal but accepts only $1/M$).

Importance sampling

The biggest lever on Monte Carlo noise: choose a sampling density $p$ shaped like the integrand so the ratio $f/p$ stays nearly constant. Shows the zero-variance ideal $p\propto f$, a ~9× reduction from one crude linear density, and why the renderer samples the GGX/cosine lobes.

Stratified sampling

Stop samples from clumping: split the domain into $N$ cells and jitter one sample into each, $x_i=(i+U_i)/N$. The variance becomes a sum of tiny within-cell pieces, so for smooth integrands it falls like $1/N^3$ (error $\propto1/N^{1.5}$) — a different convergence rate, not just a smaller constant. It's the renderer's pixel jitter.

stratificationfaster than 1/√Npixel jitter 08

Hemisphere sampling: uniform vs cosine

Where the series meets the renderer. The reflectance integral carries a $\cos\theta$ factor; sampling directions cosine-weighted ($p=\cos\theta/\pi$, via Malley's disk-lift) cancels it — zero variance for $\int\cos\theta$, and the reason a diffuse bounce weight reduces to plain albedo.

cosine-weightedMalley's methoddiffuse bounce 09

Multiple Importance Sampling (MIS)

When no single density fits the whole integrand, combine several with the balance heuristic $w_i=p_i/\sum_j p_j$. Each term collapses to the bounded $f/\sum_j p_j$, muzzling the spikes — unbiased, never much worse than the best strategy, ~2000× lower variance here, and zero when the features balance. It's the BRDF-vs-light combination of real path tracers.

Russian roulette

Terminate an infinite bounce sum after a random, finite number of steps without bias: continue with probability $q$, divide the weight by $q$ on survival. Fixed truncation is always too dark; roulette stays unbiased for any $q$ (expected length $1/(1-q)$). Choosing $q=\rho$ keeps the weight bounded — the renderer's throughput-driven termination.

unbiased terminationthroughputvariance vs cost 11

Low-discrepancy sequences (QMC)

Drop randomness for a deterministic sequence that fills space evenly at every prefix length. The van der Corput radical inverse and the Halton sequence (coprime prime bases per dimension) keep discrepancy near $1/N$, so by Koksma–Hlawka the error falls nearly as $1/N$ instead of $1/\sqrt N$ — almost squaring the accuracy. The basis of pbrt's Halton/Sobol samplers.

Sampling the normal (Box–Muller)

The Gaussian's CDF has no elementary inverse, so inversion fails. Box–Muller works in 2-D: a pair of independent normals is rotationally symmetric, so its angle is uniform and squared radius Exponential — sample those from two uniforms ($R=\sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$, $\theta=2\pi U_2$) and convert back. Verified by the histogram, the moments, and the 68–95–99.7 rule.

Sampling tabulated distributions (build & invert a CDF)

The distributions a renderer most wants to sample — HDR environment-map luminance, light powers, measured BRDFs — have no formula, only a table. Normalize it, prefix-sum it into a CDF, and binary-search for $F_i\le u<F_{i+1}$: exactly one branch-coherent step per sample (unlike rejection's variable tries), returning either an interpolated position or just an index. The machinery behind environment-map importance sampling and light selection.

tabulated pdfCDF inversionlight selection 15

Importance-sampling a 2D environment map

Lift the 1D table sampler to a 2D map by factoring the joint texel density $p(u,v)=p(v)\,p(u\mid v)$: one marginal CDF over rows, one conditional CDF per row (pbrt's Distribution2D). Two binary searches — pick a row, then a column — give one exact 2D sample, so shadow and bounce rays land on the bright sun instead of the dim sky. Verified against the joint histogram and shown cutting integral variance ~3.5×.

Adaptive sampling: spend samples where the variance is

Noise isn't spread evenly across an image, so samples shouldn't be either. Minimizing total error $\sum_i \sigma_i^2/n_i$ under a fixed budget gives the Neyman allocation $n_i\propto\sigma_i$: a cheap pilot pass estimates each pixel's standard deviation, then the rest of the budget pours into the noisy edge pixels (up to 4378 spp here) while flat interiors keep only a floor. Same ray count, 3.7× lower RMS error — with an interactive allocation toy and a live uniform-vs-adaptive render.

Resampled Importance Sampling & reservoirs (ReSTIR)

Importance-sample a target you can only evaluate, never invert — the real rendering integrand $f=\text{BRDF}\cdot L\cdot V\cdot\cos\theta$. Draw $M$ cheap candidates, weight each by the target $\hat p$, keep one survivor: unbiased at every $M$, converging to ideal importance sampling as $M$ grows. Its $O(1)$ streaming form, the weighted reservoir, is what ReSTIR reuses across neighbor pixels and frames to gather hundreds of effective samples from a few traced rays.

Importance-sampling a glossy BRDF: GGX VNDF

The series' destination — the renderer's real specular sampler. The GGX microfacet lobe is sharp and view-dependent, so uniform hemisphere sampling almost never finds it. Heitz-2018 visible-normal sampling draws a half-vector from the normals visible to the viewer and reflects, after which the Monte Carlo weight collapses to the bounded $F\,G_2/G_1$. Validated against a quadrature reference to ~3 digits and hundreds-to-thousands of times less noisy than uniform — the very SampleSpecularGGX a path tracer fires for every glossy highlight.

BRDFs & energy

Fresnel reflectance: exact vs Schlick

Every surface turns mirror-bright at grazing angles — the Fresnel ramp that multiplies every specular BRDF. The exact reflectance averages two polarizations of the electromagnetic boundary conditions (the refractive indices, or a metal's complex $n+ik$); Schlick approximates the whole curve as $F_0+(1-F_0)(1-\cos\theta)^5$ — exact at both endpoints by construction, within $0.036$ for glass and $0.015$ for the metal in between, needing only $F_0$ (from the IOR for dielectrics, the base color for metals). With a live $F(\theta)$ curve and a Fresnel-shaded sphere.

Fresnel reflectanceSchlick approximationF₀ from IOR / base color 21

The white furnace test: energy conservation

Sit a surface inside a sphere glowing with radiance $1$: outgoing radiance becomes the directional albedo $E(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H} f_r\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i$, so energy conservation is simply $E\le1$ — invisible against the furnace when $E=1$, dark when $E<1$, impossible when $E>1$. Validated on Lambertian ($E=\rho$), the test exposes that single-scatter GGX conserves energy but loses up to half of it at high roughness by ignoring multiple bounces — the deficit a Kulla–Conty term, or the renderer's $(1-F_0)$ diffuse tint, adds back.

directional albedoenergy conservationmultiple scattering 22

Refraction, Snell's law & total internal reflection

At smooth glass a ray splits: a fraction $F$ reflects, $1-F$ refracts at the Snell-bent angle $n_i\sin\theta_i=n_t\sin\theta_t$. A path tracer picks one branch with probability $F$, and the branch probability cancels the Fresnel factor — so the glass weight collapses to the same bounded $G_2/G_1$ as opaque GGX. Past the critical angle $\theta_c=\arcsin(n_t/n_i)$ Snell has no solution and all light reflects (total internal reflection). With an interactive ray-split diagram and the $F(\theta)$ curves for both directions — the skeleton of the renderer's SampleGlassGGX.

Geometry of sampling

23

Change of variables: the Jacobian, area vs solid angle

Every sampler warps uniform numbers, and the Jacobian keeps it unbiased: $p_y=p_x/|J_T|$. The "obvious" disk map $r=u$ clumps points at the center (it ignores the polar Jacobian $r$); the area-uniform fix is $r=\sqrt u$, and Shirley's concentric map is uniform and low-distortion. The same rule converts an area-light pdf to solid angle via $d\omega=\cos\theta_\ell\,dA/r^2$ — the $r^2/\cos$ factor behind next-event estimation. With three live disk samplers and a disk-light solid-angle check.

Jacobiandisk / lens samplingarea ↔ solid angle 24

Next-event estimation & light/BSDF MIS

Direct lighting can be sampled by aiming at the light (next-event estimation, pdf $r^2/(A\cos\theta_\ell)$) or by sampling the BSDF — and which one wins flips with roughness and light size, catastrophically. MIS (example 09) takes one of each and weights them by $w_i=p_i/\sum_j p_j$, staying within a small factor of the best and never the disastrous one: here the wrong single strategy costs $201\times$ in one scene and $26{,}967\times$ in the other, while MIS is robust to both. Veach's canonical result, on the two lobes a real renderer combines.

next-event estimationlight vs BSDF MISbalance / power heuristic 25

Sampling area lights: triangles & spheres

NEE needs a point on the light, and each shape is its own change of variables. A triangle uses barycentric coordinates with a square-root warp ($s=\sqrt{u_1}$, the Jacobian fix), giving $\mathbb E[b_i]=\tfrac13$ — a uniform fill whose mean is the centroid. A sphere is sampled over the cone it subtends ($\sin\theta_{\max}=R/d$, $\cos\theta=1-u_1(1-\cos\theta_{\max})$), which recovers the exact irradiance $\pi(R/d)^2$ with a ~million-fold lower variance than spraying the hemisphere. The per-shape samplers NEE calls.

Building an orthonormal basis around a normal

Every hemisphere/BRDF sampler works in a local frame with the normal at $(0,0,1)$; to use a sample you rotate it onto the real normal, $\boldsymbol\omega=\ell_x\mathbf b_1+\ell_y\mathbf b_2+\ell_z\mathbf n$, which needs two tangents. The naive cross-product breaks at the poles; Duff et al. (2017) build them branchlessly with a sign trick — $\mathbf b_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=0$ drops out of $n_x^2+n_y^2=1-n_z^2$, residual $2.9\times10^{-7}$ everywhere. Being a pure rotation, the map carries the distribution onto $\mathbf n$ untouched (cosine stays cosine, mean $\cos\theta=\tfrac23$). With a tiltable frame and rotated-sample views.
