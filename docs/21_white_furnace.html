The white furnace test: is the BRDF conserving energy?

21_white_furnace.cpp — sit a surface inside a uniformly glowing white sphere. A physical material can only reflect back what arrives; if it reflects less it looks dark, if more it has invented energy. The test that catches both — and exposes where single-scatter GGX leaks light.

A BRDF must never reflect more light than reaches it. The standard check is the white furnace test: imagine the surface inside a sphere glowing with radiance $1$ in every direction. The light it sends back is then its directional albedo $E(\omega_o)$, and physics demands $E(\omega_o)\le1$. This page derives why the furnace reads off energy conservation directly, validates the machinery on a Lambertian surface whose answer we know, and then turns it on the single-scatter GGX BRDF of example 19 — which, the test reveals, quietly loses energy as roughness grows.

One-sentence summary. Under uniform incoming radiance $L_i=1$, the rendering equation collapses to outgoing radiance $=E(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H} f_r\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i$, so an object is invisible against the furnace when $E=1$, dark when $E<1$ (lost energy), and impossible when $E>1$; estimating $E$ for single-scatter GGX (with $F=1$, just the average of $G_2/G_1$ from example 19) shows it conserves energy ($E\le1$ always) but the deficit $1-E$ climbs from under $1\%$ at $\alpha=0.05$ to nearly half the energy at $\alpha=0.8$.

1. A surface in a glowing sphere

The reflected radiance leaving a surface in direction $\omega_o$ is the rendering equation — the BRDF against all incoming light:

$$L_o(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H} f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,L_i(\omega_i)\,\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i.$$

The furnace sets $L_i(\omega_i)=1$ everywhere, so the integral loses its light term and becomes a pure property of the material — the directional albedo:

$$L_o(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H} f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i \;\equiv\; E(\omega_o).$$

That is the whole idea: in a furnace, what you see is the albedo. Now compare the object to its background, which glows at radiance $1$:

  • $E(\omega_o)=1$ → the object matches the background exactly: invisible. This is perfect energy conservation — every photon that arrives leaves.
  • $E(\omega_o)<1$ → the object is darker than the furnace; the missing $1-E$ is energy the model swallowed.
  • $E(\omega_o)>1$ → the object is brighter than its surroundings, reflecting more than arrives — physically impossible, a bug. The test's pass/fail line is exactly $E\le1$.

2. Validating the machinery: Lambertian gives $E=\rho$

Before trusting the furnace on GGX, run it on a surface whose albedo we know in closed form. A Lambertian (matte) surface has a constant BRDF $f_r=\rho/\pi$, so its directional albedo is

$$E_{\text{Lambert}}=\int_{\mathcal H}\frac{\rho}{\pi}\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i=\frac{\rho}{\pi}\int_{\mathcal H}\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i=\frac{\rho}{\pi}\cdot\pi=\rho,$$

using the hemisphere identity from example 08:

$$\int_{\mathcal H}\cos\theta\,d\omega=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\theta\,\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi=2\pi\cdot\tfrac12=\pi.$$

So a Lambertian surface of albedo $\rho$ is exactly $\rho$ as bright as the furnace — independent of view, no energy lost (and none invented). The C++ file confirms the estimator on this case: with $\rho=0.8$ it measures $E=0.8000$. The machinery is sound; now point it at GGX.

3. The GGX single-scatter deficit

Estimating $E(\omega_o)$ for GGX is already solved: it is the integral example 19 sampled with VNDF, where the weight $f_r\cos\theta_i/\text{pdf}$ collapsed to $F\,G_2/G_1$. Setting Fresnel $F=1$ (perfect mirrors, to isolate the geometry) the estimator is simply

$$E(\omega_o)\;\approx\;\frac1N\sum_{k=1}^{N}\frac{G_2(\omega_o,\omega_{i,k})}{G_1(\omega_o)},\qquad \omega_{i,k}=\text{reflect}(\omega_o,\,h_k),\ \ h_k\sim\text{VNDF}.$$

4. Where the energy went

The Smith $G_2$ term counts only light that reflects off the microsurface once and escapes without being blocked. On a rough surface a microfacet often reflects light straight into a neighbouring bump — masked, so $G_2$ discards it. But physically that ray doesn't vanish: it bounces a second or third time and usually still finds its way out. Single-scatter GGX drops every one of those multi-bounce paths, and the rougher the surface the more there are — hence the growing deficit. It is why rough metals rendered with a basic microfacet model look too dark.

Putting the energy back. The principled fix is a multiple-scattering term (Kulla–Conty 2017) that adds a compensation lobe carrying exactly the missing $1-E$, restoring $E\to1$. The PathTracingDemo renderer takes the cheaper route the table motivates: in the Standard shader it tints the diffuse lobe by $(1-F_0)$ as a rough energy-compensation step, returning some of the light the specular lobe failed to. Either way, the white furnace is the instrument that tells you how much is missing — and would flag the opposite failure ($E>1$) the moment a BRDF reflected more than it received.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 21_white_furnace.cpp.

With $F=1$ the furnace estimate of $E(\omega_o)$ is one line — the average of $G_2/G_1$ over VNDF samples (example 19's machinery, reused).

// E(wo) for GGX with F = 1, via VNDF: the average of G2/G1 over reflected samples.
double DirectionalAlbedo(const Vec3& wo, float a, Rng& rng, long long N) {
    double sum = 0.0, g1 = GgxG1(wo, a);
    for (long long i = 0; i < N; ++i) {
        Vec3 h  = SampleGgxVNDF(wo, a, rng.NextFloat(), rng.NextFloat());
        Vec3 wi = GgxReflect(wo, h);
        if (wi.z > 0.0f) sum += GgxG2(wo, wi, a) / g1;     // weight = G2/G1  (F = 1)
    }
    return sum / N;                                          // E(wo) = mean(G2/G1)
}

// Lambertian sanity check: f_r = rho/pi, so E = rho exactly.
//   E = integral_H (rho/pi) cos(theta) dwi = (rho/pi) * pi = rho.
// Energy conservation is the assertion  E(wo) <= 1  for every wo.
Takeaway. The white furnace test puts a material under uniform radiance $1$, where outgoing radiance equals the directional albedo $E(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H} f_r\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i$ — so $E\le1$ is energy conservation, readable by eye (invisible vs dark vs impossibly bright). Validated on Lambertian ($E=\rho$), it shows single-scatter GGX conserves energy but loses up to half of it at high roughness by ignoring multiple bounces — the deficit a Kulla–Conty term, or the renderer's $(1-F_0)$ diffuse tint, adds back.
