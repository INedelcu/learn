Sampling area lights: triangles & spheres

25_area_light_sampling.cpp — next-event estimation needs a point on the light, and every light shape needs its own warp: barycentric coordinates for a triangle, a cone of directions for a sphere.

Next-event estimation (example 24) only works if you can draw a good sample on the light. Each shape needs its own area-uniform map — and getting the Jacobian (example 23) right is what keeps it unbiased. Two carry most of the weight in a renderer: the triangle, the universal mesh-light primitive, and the sphere, which is far better sampled over the cone of directions it subtends than over its surface.

One-sentence summary. A uniform triangle point uses barycentric coordinates with a square-root warp — $\sqrt{u_1}$ is the Jacobian fix, giving $\mathbb E[b_i]=\tfrac13$ (the mean sample is the centroid); a sphere is sampled by aiming inside the cone of half-angle $\theta_{\max}$ ($\sin\theta_{\max}=R/d$) it subtends, with $\cos\theta=1-u_1(1-\cos\theta_{\max})$ and pdf $1/\bigl(2\pi(1-\cos\theta_{\max})\bigr)$ — which recovers the exact irradiance $I=\pi(R/d)^2$ with vastly lower variance than firing rays at the whole hemisphere.

1. To light a point, sample the light

Direct lighting (example 24) integrates over the light's surface, so it needs samples drawn on that surface — area-uniform at least, solid-angle-aware where it helps. Each light shape is a different change of variables from the unit square $[0,1)^2$, and each carries a Jacobian that must be respected or the samples are biased toward one part of the light. Here are the two workhorses.

2. Triangles: barycentric coordinates with a square root

A point in a triangle is a barycentric blend $P=b_0A+b_1B+b_2C$ with $b_0+b_1+b_2=1$. The area-uniform map from two uniforms is

$$s=\sqrt{u_1},\qquad b_0=1-s,\qquad b_1=u_2\,s,\qquad b_2=1-b_0-b_1.$$

The square root is the Jacobian fix from example 23 — using $s=u_1$ instead bunches points toward vertex $A$, because equal steps in $b_0$ cover unequal areas of the triangle. That the warp is correct is easy to verify in expectation: with $s=\sqrt{u_1}$,

$$\mathbb E[b_0]=\mathbb E[1-\sqrt{u_1}]=1-\!\int_0^1\!\sqrt u\,du=1-\tfrac23=\tfrac13,\qquad \mathbb E[b_1]=\mathbb E[u_2]\,\mathbb E[\sqrt{u_1}]=\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac23=\tfrac13,$$

and $\mathbb E[b_2]=\tfrac13$ by subtraction. All three average $\tfrac13$, so the mean sample is the centroid $(A+B+C)/3$ — the signature of a uniform fill. The C++ run confirms it over 4M samples: mean barycentric $(0.3334,\,0.3333,\,0.3333)$, mean position $(0.6657,0.9989,0)$ against the centroid $(0.6667,1,0)$. 3. Spheres: sample the cone, not the surface

For a sphere you could sample its surface, but most of it faces away or misses the shading point's view. Far better: a sphere of radius $R$ seen from distance $d$ fills a cone of half-angle $\theta_{\max}$ with $\sin\theta_{\max}=R/d$, and you sample directions uniformly inside that cone. Uniform in solid angle means the density is constant, $1/\Omega$, over $\Omega=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^{\theta_{\max}}\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi=2\pi(1-\cos\theta_{\max})$. Since $d\omega=\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi$, uniform solid angle makes $\cos\theta$ uniform on $[\cos\theta_{\max},1]$, so by inversion (example 05):

$$\cos\theta=1-u_1\,(1-\cos\theta_{\max}),\qquad \phi=2\pi u_2,\qquad p(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi(1-\cos\theta_{\max})}.$$

To test it, estimate the unshadowed irradiance from an overhead sphere of unit radiance, $I=\int_{\text{cone}}\cos\theta\,d\omega$. That integral has a closed form:

$$I=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^{\theta_{\max}}\cos\theta\,\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi =2\pi\cdot\tfrac12\sin^2\theta_{\max}=\pi\sin^2\theta_{\max}=\pi\Big(\frac{R}{d}\Big)^2.$$

Cone sampling hits the answer ($I=\pi(R/d)^2=0.125664$ for $R/d=0.2$) with tiny variance, because $\cos\theta$ barely varies inside a small cone. Uniform-hemisphere sampling, by contrast, only contributes when a direction happens to fall in the cone — a fraction $1-\cos\theta_{\max}$ of the hemisphere — so almost every sample is wasted. 4. In the renderer

These are the per-shape samplers next-event estimation calls. The triangle map samples mesh lights and emissive geometry — any light a modeler builds is triangles, and the $\sqrt{u_1}$ warp is what keeps an emissive quad or a glowing model uniformly sampled. Cone sampling handles spherical and disk lights, and its solid-angle pdf $1/(2\pi(1-\cos\theta_{\max}))$ plugs straight into the light-vs-BSDF MIS of example 24. A renderer with many lights first picks one (the discrete sampler of example 14), then samples a point on it with exactly these maps.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 25_area_light_sampling.cpp.

Each sampler is a few lines: the triangle's square-root barycentrics, and the cone's $\cos\theta$ inversion.

// TRIANGLE: area-uniform barycentric point (the sqrt is the Jacobian fix).
float su = std::sqrt(u1);
float b0 = 1.0f - su, b1 = u2 * su, b2 = 1.0f - b0 - b1;
Vec3 P = A*b0 + B*b1 + C*b2;                       // E[b_i] = 1/3 -> centroid

// SPHERE: sample the cone it subtends (sin theta_max = R/d).
double cosMax = std::sqrt(1.0 - (R/d)*(R/d));
double ct  = 1.0 - u1 * (1.0 - cosMax);            // cos(theta), uniform in [cosMax,1]
double st  = std::sqrt(1.0 - ct*ct), ph = 2*PI*u2;
Vec3 wc(st*std::cos(ph), st*std::sin(ph), ct);     // direction toward the sphere
double pdf = 1.0 / (2*PI * (1.0 - cosMax));        // uniform solid angle in the cone
// estimating I = integral cos(theta) dω over the cone gives pi*(R/d)^2 exactly.
Takeaway. Every light shape is a change of variables. A triangle is barycentric with $s=\sqrt{u_1}$, the Jacobian fix that makes $\mathbb E[b_i]=\tfrac13$ (uniform fill, mean = centroid). A sphere is best sampled over the cone it subtends — $\cos\theta=1-u_1(1-\cos\theta_{\max})$, pdf $1/(2\pi(1-\cos\theta_{\max}))$ — which recovers the exact irradiance $\pi(R/d)^2$ with up to a million-fold less variance than spraying the hemisphere. These are the samplers next-event estimation (24) calls once it has chosen a light.
