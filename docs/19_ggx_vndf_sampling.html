Importance-sampling a glossy BRDF: GGX with VNDF

19_ggx_vndf_sampling.cpp — the series' destination. The renderer's specular sampler: draw a microfacet normal from the normals visible to the viewer (Heitz 2018), reflect, and the Monte Carlo weight collapses to F·G2/G1.

Everything so far has been building toward this. Importance sampling said: shape your sampling density like the integrand. Hemisphere sampling did it for the cosine lobe. Real glossy reflection is the hard case — the GGX microfacet BRDF concentrates outgoing energy into a tight lobe around the mirror direction, and that lobe moves with the view. Firing rays uniformly over the hemisphere almost never lands in it, so the variance is catastrophic. The fix the renderer actually uses is visible-normal (VNDF) sampling, and its payoff is an almost magical cancellation in the Monte Carlo weight.

One-sentence summary. The microfacet BRDF is $f_r=\dfrac{D(h)\,G_2\,F}{4\,(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)(n\!\cdot\!\omega_i)}$; VNDF sampling draws a half-vector $h$ from the distribution of microfacet normals visible from $\omega_o$ and reflects $\omega_o$ about it, after which the weight $f_r\cos\theta_i/\text{pdf}$ collapses to the bounded, cheap $\;F\,G_2(\omega_o,\omega_i)/G_1(\omega_o)\;$ (Heitz 2018, eq. 19) — orders of magnitude less variance than uniform sampling, the gap widening as the surface gets smoother.

1. Why glossy reflection is hard

Roughness $\alpha$ controls how tightly the microfacet normals cluster around the surface normal (the renderer uses $\alpha=(1-\text{smoothness})^2$). Small $\alpha$ means a near-mirror: the reflected lobe is a needle pointed at the mirror direction. The plot below is that lobe — the BRDF times $\cos\theta_i$ as a function of outgoing direction in the plane of incidence. Drag the roughness and the view angle: the lobe sharpens toward a spike as $\alpha\to 0$ and swings to follow the mirror direction as the view tilts. A sampler that ignores this — uniform over the hemisphere — puts almost none of its samples under the spike.

2. The microfacet BRDF

A glossy surface is modeled as a field of tiny perfect mirrors (microfacets). Two functions describe it:

  • $D(h)$ — the normal distribution: how many microfacets are oriented with normal $h$. For GGX, $D$ is sharply peaked at $h=n$ for small $\alpha$. A microfacet reflects $\omega_o$ into $\omega_i$ only if its normal is the half-vector $h=\widehat{\omega_o+\omega_i}$.
  • $G_2(\omega_o,\omega_i)$ — the Smith masking-shadowing term: the fraction of those microfacets that are neither hidden from the viewer nor in shadow from the light. $G_1$ is the one-sided version (masking from $\omega_o$ alone).
$$f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)=\frac{D(h)\,G_2(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,F}{4\,(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)(n\!\cdot\!\omega_i)},\qquad h=\widehat{\omega_o+\omega_i}.$$

$F$ is the Fresnel reflectance; we set $F=1$ here to isolate the geometry. The sharp, view-dependent factor is $D$ — exactly the part we must sample well.

3. Visible-normal sampling and the magic weight

The naive idea is to sample $h\propto D(h)$ and reflect. VNDF (Heitz 2018) does better: it samples $h$ from the microfacet normals actually visible from $\omega_o$ — facets pointing away from the viewer can never contribute, so sampling them is wasted. Mechanically: stretch $\omega_o$ by $\alpha$, sample a point on the projected hemisphere disk, and unstretch. Then $\omega_i=\text{reflect}(\omega_o,h)$.

The reward is what happens to the estimator weight. Writing out $f_r\cos\theta_i$ divided by the VNDF pdf of $\omega_i$, the $D$, the Jacobian of the reflection, and the cosines all cancel, leaving

$$\text{weight}=\frac{f_r\cos\theta_i}{\text{pdf}(\omega_i)}=\frac{F\,G_2(\omega_o,\omega_i)}{G_1(\omega_o)}.$$
Why it collapses. Two ingredients do it. Heitz's distribution of visible normals is

$$D_{\text{vis}}(h)=\frac{G_1(\omega_o)\,(\omega_o\!\cdot\!h)\,D(h)}{n\!\cdot\!\omega_o},$$

and reflecting $\omega_o$ about $h$ carries the half-vector Jacobian $\big|\partial\omega_h/\partial\omega_i\big|=1/\big(4\,(\omega_o\!\cdot\!h)\big)$, so the pdf in the outgoing direction is

$$\text{pdf}(\omega_i)=\frac{D_{\text{vis}}(h)}{4\,(\omega_o\!\cdot\!h)}=\frac{G_1(\omega_o)\,D(h)}{4\,(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)} .$$

Meanwhile the numerator, using $\cos\theta_i=n\!\cdot\!\omega_i$, is $f_r\cos\theta_i=\dfrac{D(h)\,G_2\,F}{4\,(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)}$. Divide:

$$\frac{f_r\cos\theta_i}{\text{pdf}(\omega_i)}=\frac{D(h)\,G_2\,F\big/\big(4(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)\big)}{G_1(\omega_o)\,D(h)\big/\big(4(n\!\cdot\!\omega_o)\big)}=\frac{F\,G_2(\omega_o,\omega_i)}{G_1(\omega_o)} .$$

The spiky $D(h)$, both cosines, the factor $4$, and the $(\omega_o\!\cdot\!h)$ from the Jacobian all cancel — that is the whole point of sampling the visible normals. (The §4 table confirms it numerically: the average of $G_2/G_1$ matches the BRDF quadrature to three digits.)

No $D$, no division by tiny cosines, no chance of a spike — just a bounded ratio of Smith terms, near 1. Below, watch where the two samplers send their rays: VNDF clusters under the lobe, uniform scatters everywhere.

4. It's correct, and it crushes the variance

Two claims to verify: that the $F\,G_2/G_1$ weight is right, and that it pays off. The C++ file estimates the single-scatter directional albedo $E(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H} f_r\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i$ (with $F=1$) by VNDF — simply the average of $G_2/G_1$ — and checks it against a deterministic quadrature of the hemisphere. They agree to ~3 digits, and the VNDF variance is hundreds to thousands of times below uniform's:

5. The leftover: $E(\omega_o)<1$

Notice every albedo above is less than 1, and it drops as roughness rises (to ~0.59 at $\alpha=0.6$). A perfectly reflecting surface ($F=1$) should return all its energy, so where did it go? The Smith $G_2$ term only accounts for light masked or shadowed on a single bounce off the microsurface; the energy that bounces between microfacets two or more times is dropped. That missing fraction is the single-scatter energy loss, and it is exactly why rough metals rendered with a basic microfacet BRDF look too dark. Recovering it (multiple-scattering / Kulla–Conty compensation) is measured by a white-furnace test — the natural next step beyond this series.

6. In the renderer

This is not a toy: the SampleGgxVNDF, GgxG1/GgxG2, and the $F\,G_2/G_1$ weight in this example mirror the renderer's real specular path. In PathTracingDemo, the function SampleSpecularGGX in BRDF.hlsl samples a visible normal, reflects the view vector, and returns precisely this weight — the same Heitz-2018 routine, just in HLSL on the GPU. Every glossy highlight the renderer produces is this sampler keeping the variance finite.

That closes the loop. The series began with a coin-flip estimator and the $1/\sqrt N$ law; it ends with the exact importance sampler a production path tracer fires for specular reflection. Random variables, variance, the $f/p$ estimator, importance sampling, stratification, low-discrepancy sequences, RIS — every idea was a step toward placing this one ray well.

7. The code

The estimator is the punchline: with VNDF, the specular Monte Carlo weight is just two Smith terms.

// VNDF estimator of E(wo): draw a visible normal, reflect, weight = G2/G1 (F = 1).
Vec3 hv  = SampleGgxVNDF(wo, a, rng.NextFloat(), rng.NextFloat());  // visible half-vector
Vec3 wiv = GgxReflect(wo, hv);                                       // omega_i
double w = (wiv.z > 0.0f) ? GgxG2(wo, wiv, a) / GgxG1(wo, a) : 0.0;  // Heitz 2018, eq. 19

// the Smith terms it is built from:
float GgxG1(const Vec3& v, float a)              { return 1.0f / (1.0f + GgxLambda(v, a)); }
float GgxG2(const Vec3& wo, const Vec3& wi, float a) {
    return 1.0f / (1.0f + GgxLambda(wo, a) + GgxLambda(wi, a));      // height-correlated Smith
}
Takeaway. Glossy reflection has a sharp, view-dependent lobe that uniform sampling can't find. Sampling the GGX visible normals and reflecting puts the ray where the energy is, and the Monte Carlo weight collapses to the bounded $F\,G_2/G_1$ — validated against quadrature to three digits and hundreds-to-thousands of times less noisy than uniform, most of all on smooth surfaces. It is the importance-sampling principle of example 06, applied to the one integral a path tracer evaluates most.
