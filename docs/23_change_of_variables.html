Change of variables: the Jacobian, area vs solid angle

23_change_of_variables.cpp — every sampler warps uniform numbers into points or directions, and the Jacobian is the bookkeeping that keeps the density honest. Get it wrong and your lens, area-light, and BRDF samples are quietly biased.

Every sampling routine in this series — inversion, cosine hemispheres, GGX, environment maps — is a change of variables: feed in uniform numbers, warp them into the shape you want. The rule that keeps the result unbiased is the Jacobian: when you stretch space, the density has to shrink by the same factor. Skip it and you get a silent bias — the classic example being the disk map that looks right and piles every sample at the center. This page is the geometry that underlies the next few: disk and lens sampling, and the area-to-solid-angle conversion that next-event estimation runs on.

One-sentence summary. A map $y=T(x)$ transforms a density by $p_y(y)=p_x(x)/|J_T(x)|$ where $|J_T|$ is how much $T$ stretches area/volume; the polar map's Jacobian is $r$, so uniform $(r,\phi)$ clumps at the disk center and the area-uniform radius is $r=\sqrt{u}$; and the area↔solid-angle Jacobian $d\omega=\cos\theta_\ell\,dA/r^2$ turns an area-light pdf into a solid-angle pdf via the factor $r^2/\cos\theta_\ell$ — the geometry term behind next-event estimation.

naive $r=u$ (biased) correct $r=\sqrt{u}$ concentric (Shirley) area ↔ solid angle

1. Every sampler is a change of variables

Inversion (example 05) took $U\sim[0,1)$ and warped it through the inverse CDF. Box–Muller (example 12) warped two uniforms into the plane. Every sampler is a map $y=T(x)$, and the law that keeps densities consistent under it is the change-of-variables formula:

$$y=T(x),\quad X\sim p_x \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; p_y(y)=\frac{p_x(x)}{|J_T(x)|},\qquad |J_T(x)|=\left|\det\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|.$$

The Jacobian $|J_T|$ is the local stretch factor — the ratio of output volume to input volume. Where the map expands space, samples spread out and the density drops; where it compresses, they bunch and the density rises. The whole job of getting a sampler right is tracking that factor; getting it wrong tilts the density without any error message.

2. Sampling a disk: the trap and the fix

To sample a disk it is tempting to write $r=u_1$, $\phi=2\pi u_2$. It is wrong. The polar map $(x,y)=(r\cos\phi,\,r\sin\phi)$ has Jacobian

$$\left|\det\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\phi)}\right| =\left|\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\phi & -r\sin\phi\\ \sin\phi & r\cos\phi\end{pmatrix}\right|=r,$$

so feeding it uniform $(r,\phi)$ gives a disk density $p(x,y)=p(r,\phi)/r\propto 1/r$ — diverging at the center. The points pile into the middle. To make the disk area-uniform ($p(x,y)=1/\pi$) the radius must instead be drawn with density $p(r)\propto r$. Its CDF is the enclosed-area fraction,

$$F(r)=\frac{\pi r^2}{\pi\,1^2}=r^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; r=\sqrt{u}\quad(\text{inversion, example 05}),$$

which is the one-character fix: $r=\sqrt{u}$. Shirley's concentric map reaches the same uniform density a different way — it maps the square $[-1,1]^2$ to the disk in wedges — and is additionally low-distortion: neighbouring square samples stay neighbours on the disk, so a stratified or low-discrepancy grid (example 16) survives the warp, which $r=\sqrt u$ scrambles. Below, $1500$ points each way; the metric is the fraction landing in the inner half-radius (uniform → $25\%$, since that inner disk is a quarter of the area).

3. Area vs solid angle: the $r^2/\cos$ factor

The same idea, in 3-D, connects two ways to integrate light from an area source. A small patch of light of area $dA$, sitting a distance $r$ away with its normal tilted at angle $\theta_\ell$ to the line of sight, presents a foreshortened area $dA\cos\theta_\ell$; dividing by $r^2$ (inverse-square falloff) gives the solid angle it covers at the shading point:

$$d\omega=\frac{\cos\theta_\ell}{r^2}\,dA.$$

That is the Jacobian between the area measure and the solid-angle measure. Equating probability mass under the two, $p_\omega\,d\omega=p_A\,dA$, converts one density to the other:

$$p_\omega=p_A\,\frac{dA}{d\omega}=p_A\,\frac{r^2}{\cos\theta_\ell}.$$

So a sample drawn uniformly on a light's area ($p_A=1/A$) has solid-angle density $p_\omega=r^2/(A\cos\theta_\ell)$ — the exact factor next-event estimation (the pdf MIS needs) uses to weigh an area-light sample against a BRDF sample. To check the conversion, the file estimates the solid angle a disk light subtends by sampling its area and multiplying by $r^2/\cos\theta_\ell$, against a quadrature reference and the on-axis closed form.

4. In the renderer

Both halves are everyday renderer geometry. The disk map is how a path tracer samples a thin lens for depth of field (jitter the ray origin over the aperture disk) and how it samples a disk-shaped light — and it must be the area-uniform $r=\sqrt u$ (or concentric), or the bokeh and the light both brighten toward their centers. The $r^2/\cos\theta_\ell$ factor is the bridge that lets the renderer aim a ray at a light and still weigh it correctly against a BRDF-sampled ray: that is next-event estimation, the subject of example 24, where this conversion turns an area sample into the solid-angle pdf MIS compares. Get the Jacobian right here and those two pieces compose without bias.

5. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 23_change_of_variables.cpp.

The correct disk radius is one sqrt; the area→solid-angle conversion is one $r^2/\cos$ factor on each area sample.

// area-uniform disk: the Jacobian of polar coords is r, so draw r with CDF r^2.
float r   = std::sqrt(rng.NextFloat());        // r = sqrt(u)  (NOT r = u)
float phi = 2.0f * kPi * rng.NextFloat();
float x = r * std::cos(phi), y = r * std::sin(phi);

// estimate the solid angle a disk light subtends by sampling its AREA (p = 1/A)
// and converting each sample to solid-angle measure with r^2 / cos(theta_light):
double sum = 0.0;
for (int i = 0; i < M; ++i) {
    float rr = R * std::sqrt(rng.NextFloat()), ph = 2*kPi*rng.NextFloat();   // area-uniform
    double px = rr*std::cos(ph), py = rr*std::sin(ph);
    double r  = std::sqrt(px*px + py*py + H*H);     // distance to the sample
    double cosLight = H / r;                        // light faces the point
    sum += cosLight / (r*r);                        // integrand  cos/r^2
}
double omega = sum * (A / M);                       // E[cos/r^2] * A  ==  Omega
Takeaway. Sampling is warping uniform numbers, and the Jacobian $p_y=p_x/|J_T|$ is what keeps the density correct under the warp. The polar Jacobian $r$ is why area-uniform disk sampling needs $r=\sqrt u$, not $r=u$ — the difference between even bokeh and a bright blob. The area↔solid-angle Jacobian $d\omega=\cos\theta_\ell\,dA/r^2$ gives the $r^2/\cos\theta_\ell$ factor that converts an area-light pdf to solid angle, the geometric heart of next-event estimation.
